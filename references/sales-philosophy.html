Sales Philosophy

Core Principle: Environment Over Pressure

"Don't try to get people to buy. Create an environment and process that makes people want to buy. We don't need to make the sale, but create something that people can't help but buy." — Tim, New sales thoughts

"I'm going to treat you that you'll only buy if it's right for you. Hopefully you respect the fact that I don't do high pressure sales, because I believe that the people I talk to are smart enough to make their own decision." — Tim, New sales thoughts

"If it doesn't make sense to you, then don't do it. I care too much about you that I don't want you to do something you're not comfortable with. I have no desire for you to buy something that isn't right for you." — Tim, New sales thoughts

Resistance to Persistence

"If you get into a resistance to persistence situation, you've missed the mark. Ask questions, find holes, show how this plugs the holes, explain it in a way that they realise that it's a much better deal for them than it is for you." — Tim, New sales thoughts

Pain and Pleasure Discovery

"I need to know your pain, and then determine whether my offer will help quell that unique pain. And I need to understand your pleasure, so we can make sure this will move you towards that." — Tim, New sales thoughts

"Don't talk to a cold audience about a positive outcome. Instead, warn a cold audience about a negative outcome." — Tim, New sales thoughts

Robert Cialdini's research on "pre-suasion" showed that framing a message around loss prevention is significantly more persuasive than framing it around gain -- especially with audiences who have not yet established trust. Loss-framed messaging activates urgency in a way that gain-framed messaging cannot match for cold prospects. — Robert Cialdini, Pre-Suasion: A Revolutionary Way to Influence and Persuade (2016)

Process vs Offer

"Your process is not your offer. If you show the process first, they associate it with difficulty." — Tim, New sales thoughts

Questions in every prospect's mind:

  1. Will this work?
  2. Is this true?
  3. Will this work for me?

"You need to cover these off." — Tim

Funnel Framework

The structure of any effective sales conversation or marketing asset:

  1. Hook -- Capture attention through pain, process, or prize
  2. Value Prop -- What will you get if you engage? How we solve that problem
  3. Credibility -- Why you should trust us (how long in business, value of work, awards, qualifications, research, testimonials)
  4. Call to Action -- How to get started

Pricing Confidence

"No means next. If you get three yeses in a row, raise prices by at least 20%."

Three consecutive yeses is a signal that you're underpriced. The market is telling you there's room.

Pricing researcher Hermann Simon found that most service businesses systematically underprice by 20-30%, and that the fear of losing a sale causes more revenue damage than actually losing sales to higher prices. His research showed that a 1% price increase, if volume holds, typically produces an 8-11% increase in operating profit. — Hermann Simon, Confessions of the Pricing Man (2015)

Self-Convincing Questions

"Ask questions to have someone defend why they want something they don't have. They convince themselves."

Don't argue for the sale. Get the prospect to articulate their own gap -- the distance between where they are and where they want to be. When they hear themselves say it, the conviction is theirs.

This technique is central to motivational interviewing, developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick. Their clinical research demonstrated that when people voice their own reasons for change (called "change talk"), they are far more likely to follow through than when arguments are presented to them externally. Self-generated motivation is more durable than externally imposed motivation. — William R. Miller & Stephen Rollnick, Motivational Interviewing (3rd ed., 2012)

The Spectrum of Certainty

"Desire leads to certainty process leads to production." — Tim

The decision-making production line: Idea -> Question -> Hypothesis -> Necessity -> Canon. What question in your thinking are you avoiding? Creativity is not about finding the answers, it's finding the questions.

Perception vs Perspective

"Your perception doesn't add any value, it's your interpretation of the world around you, unique only to you. There's no advantage in your perception. Perspective is observing the world from outside of yourself -- putting yourself in the shoes of the person you're dealing with. What are they worried about, how do they feel, what are their stressors. When you are thinking like them, you have an informational advantage." — Tim

This is the core of sales mastery: stop seeing through your own eyes and start seeing through theirs. Perception is self-centred. Perspective is client-centred.

Adam Grant's research on "perspective-taking" in negotiations and sales found that the most successful negotiators were those who understood the other party's thoughts and interests (cognitive empathy), not just their feelings (emotional empathy). Perspective-takers secured better outcomes for both sides and built longer-lasting relationships. — Adam Grant, Give and Take (2013)
