Trust Equation

From Conference25 - High Performing Teams session:

Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Interest

Building Each Element

Credibility

  • Do you know what you're talking about?
  • Can you demonstrate expertise?

Reliability

  • Do you do what you say you'll do?
  • Are you consistent?

Intimacy

  • Surface level won't cut it
  • "What are you doing on the weekend?" → "How was the weekend?" is too surface level
  • Need to go deeper to build real intimacy

Brene Brown's research on vulnerability found that trust is built not through perfection but through the willingness to be genuine. In professional relationships, sharing appropriate vulnerability signals that you are invested in the person, not just the transaction. Intimacy in the trust equation requires moving past pleasantries to what actually matters. — Brene Brown, Daring Greatly (2012)

Minimising Self-Interest

  • The denominator - reduces trust when high
  • Be committed vs attached
  • "Attached is like you're trying to get something out of it. Committed to the relationship, focus on adding value."

Trust and Teams

High trust enables healthy conflict. The right conversations to reconfirm alignment.

Don't aim for consensus but commitment. What is happening, who is doing it, what I'm on the hook for.

What's the difference between commitment and accountability? Consequence.

The Trust Footrace (from The trust footrace)

Before we set out we need to categorise the nature of the relationship - short or long term.

It's all about pacing:

  • Short term: Deliver all reason to trust right now. It's transactional.
  • Long term: Pace like a marathon. Deliver value slowly, continue to do what you promise.

"Don't go out too hard, otherwise you risk the other person becoming accustomed to a higher level of personal touch, only to be inevitably let down when it doesn't continue."

The trust gradient gives context to Under promise over deliver.

Trust as Currency

"Trust is earned in drops, and lost in buckets." — Tim

"When people want to believe something, they'll fall for anything." — Tim

The asymmetry of trust is brutal. Years of careful reliability can be undone by a single moment of carelessness. This is why consistency isn't optional -- it's the entire game.

Nassim Taleb uses the metaphor of "a thousand days cannot prove you right, but one day can prove you wrong" to describe this asymmetry. In trust, as in risk management, the distribution is heavily skewed: positive events accumulate slowly and linearly, while negative events strike suddenly and catastrophically. — Nassim Nicholas Taleb, The Black Swan (2007)
