PEACE Framework

From Conference25 - Stress and Client Experience session.

For anticipating and managing client stress.

P - Prepare

  • Be across the detail
  • Make sure the client knows what to expect from the meeting

E - Establish Empathy

  • Provide space to acknowledge stress before getting down to business
  • Be careful of comparison even if trying to build rapport
  • Instead, mirror their language (e.g., how they refer to the deceased - did they say "die" or "pass away"? If they say "off the perch", say "off the perch")

Linguistic mirroring is supported by FBI negotiation research. Chris Voss found that repeating the last 1-3 words of what someone has said (or using their exact terminology) creates an unconscious sense of rapport and signals that you are truly listening. In emotionally charged situations, mirroring is more effective than paraphrasing because it preserves the client's own frame of reference. — Chris Voss, Never Split the Difference (2016)

A - Active Awareness

  • "That sounds really tough", NOT "I understand"
  • You risk being an "accidental thief" of their experience
  • Avoid inflicted cheer and "bright siding"
  • "It's OK, take your time"

C - Considerate Control

  • Choice overload can be toxic
  • Check in with clients
  • Let them choose the time, pace, method of communication

Sheena Iyengar's famous "jam study" demonstrated that people presented with 24 options were 10 times less likely to purchase than those presented with 6. In high-stress situations, the paralysis effect of too many choices is amplified. Offering a curated set of options -- not an exhaustive menu -- respects the client's cognitive bandwidth during difficult moments. — Sheena Iyengar & Mark Lepper, "When Choice is Demotivating," Journal of Personality and Social Psychology (2000)

E - Ease into the End

  • Soft landings
  • Avoid abrupt finishes
  • Avoid overly scripted closure
  • Try to personalise and recap
  • Offer support resources

Key Insight

It's not prescriptive, it's dynamic.

Ask them to replay in their own words what they've heard you say.

Application: When Someone Is in Crisis

"When someone is in crisis -- do you want to talk about it or be distracted from it?" — Tim

Not everyone in crisis wants to process. Some need space, some need distraction. The PEACE framework provides the structure to read the room and adapt.

"Black and white thinking shows you are being emotional. But often the person thinks they are being logical." — Tim

Recognising when a client has shifted from rational to emotional -- without telling them that's what's happened -- is a core PEACE skill. The approach is to gently slow down, not to confront.

Daniel Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 framework explains this: when a client shifts to black-and-white thinking, they have moved from deliberate, analytical System 2 processing to fast, emotional System 1 processing. Telling someone they are being emotional triggers defensiveness. Instead, slowing the conversation pace naturally re-engages System 2. — Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011)
