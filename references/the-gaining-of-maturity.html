The Gaining of Maturity

"I think a sign of maturity is that you become ashamed of views you once tightly held." — Tim

This happens as we evolve our perspective and think for ourselves. Often times, our beliefs are really not ours at all, but birthed by someone else. That's not to say any one of them is inherently incorrect, but they should be thoroughly examined, explored and weighed.

It can be hard to do so with true diligence. After all, we are talking to the foundations of who we are. But that is the point -- who we are shouldn't be based on the views of another. We are unique, but somehow thwart such majesty.

Developmental psychologist Robert Kegan describes this as the shift from a "socialised mind" (where our beliefs are authored by our environment) to a "self-authoring mind" (where we take ownership of our own meaning-making). Most adults never complete this transition. — Robert Kegan, The Evolving Self: Problem and Process in Human Development (1982)

On Catching Yourself

"You have to try to catch yourself. Hold beliefs at arm's length. And see there are different ones you can choose. It all starts by thinking you might be wrong, even if your instincts are telling you it's right. If I were wrong, how would that look? It's cognitive flexibility." — Mark Manson

"In what way is this activity or habit supporting my values?" — Tim

Maturity isn't just shedding old beliefs. It's building the habit of questioning current ones. The mature mind holds beliefs at arm's length -- close enough to use, far enough to inspect.

Adam Grant calls this "thinking like a scientist" — treating your own beliefs as hypotheses to be tested rather than truths to be defended. The people who update their beliefs most readily also tend to be the most successful forecasters and decision-makers. — Adam Grant, Think Again: The Power of Knowing What You Don't Know (2021)

On Feelings That Change Over Time

"Feelings once felt change over time." — Tim

What devastated you at 20 might seem quaint at 40. What motivated you at 30 might bore you at 50. Maturity is understanding that your emotional relationship to events is not fixed -- it evolves, and that evolution is itself the growing up.

Psychologist Dan Gilbert's research on "affective forecasting" shows we consistently overestimate how long emotional states will last. We have a "psychological immune system" that neutralises negative experiences faster than we predict. What feels permanent at 20 is already fading by 25. — Daniel Gilbert, Stumbling on Happiness (2006)

On Living With Contradictions

"Just because you don't know how you function doesn't mean you are broken." — Tim

"Explains a lot but doesn't change a thing." — Tim

Part of gaining maturity is accepting that understanding your patterns doesn't automatically fix them. Insight is step one. But it's not the destination. The mature person can hold the tension between knowing why they do something and still doing it -- and not beating themselves up while they work on changing it.

This is the "insight fallacy" — the assumption that understanding a problem is the same as solving it. Cognitive behavioural research shows that insight alone changes behaviour in fewer than 15% of cases; lasting change requires repeated practice of new behaviours, not just awareness of old ones. — James Prochaska et al., Changing for Good (1994)
