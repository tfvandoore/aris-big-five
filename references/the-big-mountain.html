The big mountain

The Big Mountain

"Make sure you know what achievement looks like. Otherwise it will just pass you by." -- Tim

The Story

This was something Nick mentioned in a workshop:

A young boy looks out the window and says, "One day, I'm going to be like my Dad, and climb to the top of that mountain." One day he does it -- at say 8 years old. He's very proud of himself. Wow, I did it.

If he stopped there, that's what achievement would look and feel like. Wow.

He grows up a bit and is a teenager. One day he realises it is just a hill. He thinks, "Ahhhh, that wasn't anything. It was just a hill."

Then upon reflection, we recognise that it was a mountain when I was 8. I'm proud of it. I should have been proud of it when I was 16 too. Now I'm an adult, I should still look back at that and remember it as a cool moment.

The Lesson

"Remember it as a big hill. The big pineapple was big and scary. Don't let learned cynicism dilute what you were justifiably proud of." -- Tim

Achievement is relative to where you were when you achieved it. The danger is that as you grow, your frame of reference shifts, and suddenly the things that once felt monumental feel trivial. But they weren't trivial at the time. That 8-year-old climbed a mountain. The fact that it looks smaller from up here doesn't change what it was.

This reflects what psychologists call "hedonic adaptation" -- our tendency to return to a baseline level of satisfaction regardless of achievements, which causes past accomplishments to feel smaller over time. The antidote is deliberate gratitude and recognition of past effort relative to past capacity. - Sonja Lyubomirsky, The How of Happiness (2007)

The Cynicism Trap

This is Level Up Mindset in reverse. We level up, and then we look back at our earlier levels with contempt. "That was nothing." But it wasn't nothing. It was everything -- at that level. And dismissing past achievements is a way of robbing yourself of the confidence they should have built.

"Confidence comes from the past, not the present."

If you rewrite your past achievements as insignificant, you delete the evidence your confidence is built on. Every big mountain you climbed at 8 is a proof point. Don't tear them down just because you can see further now.

Carol Dweck's research on mindset shows that people with a growth mindset celebrate the process and effort of past achievements, while those with a fixed mindset discount them -- and this dismissal actively undermines future confidence and willingness to take on new challenges. - Carol Dweck, Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (2006)

Applied to Business and Career

The first client meeting that went well. The first deal that closed. The first time you led a team meeting without your notes. These were mountains. The temptation, years later, is to dismiss them. But the person who honours their past mountains climbs future ones with more conviction.

"Better past, bigger future." -- Tim

Applied to Parenting

Kids need to be allowed to have their mountains. A child's first school presentation is their Everest. Don't shrink it for them. And don't shrink it for yourself later either. Their mountains are real, and so were yours.
