Identity and Self

On Who We Are

"You are not the same person as your former self. Don't hate on your former self, give it credit for doing the best that it could with what it had."

"Most identities are defined by who they were in the past, not who they will be in the future. Your identity is driven by the story you tell yourself about yourself, so tell yourself the story about where you're going, not where you've been."

Hal Hershfield's neuroimaging research showed that people think about their future selves using the same brain regions they use to think about strangers -- suggesting that building a vivid connection to your future identity is critical for long-term motivation. - Hal Hershfield, Your Future Self (2023)

"You can choose to identify with what you know (ideologue) or with what you don't know (a seeker of truth)"

On Change

"Don't fake yourself out that who you are today is who you will be in the future"

"You don't have to choose between who you are and who you used to be"

"You need to let go of who you've been to become who you need to be"

"Ambition isn't arrogant, it's transformative. We're always becoming. The question is, becoming what?"

On Self-Knowledge

"You don't start to know yourself until you stop making excuses"

"Reflect on what you find irritating in others. It will give you a path for personal development"

"High levels of self suspicion. High levels of self awareness"

"We are all becoming. Becoming who. Becoming what. Becoming why."

On Self-Worth

"I don't have to do something to be something/loved"

"If you only love you 20%, finding someone who loves you 30% feels like winning the lottery"

"How I view myself is of utmost importance"

On Letting Go of the Past Self

"Don't be so attached to who you are now that you don't give future you a chance." - Tim

"At some stage, everyone has to be their own man." - Tim

"Do you define yourself by your past, or your future?" - Tim

"I've unearthed a version of myself that I kinda like." - Tim

The gravity of all of our yesterdays holds us back. But Gravity is weak. That weight comes from past motivations, habits, identity, and self-concept. As we let go of the beliefs that no longer serve us, we start to jump higher -- like astronauts on the moon.

On Self-Deception and Honesty

"You might be able to conceptualise what you want, but emotionally you feel you don't deserve it." - Tim

"Your unconscious will only allow you to receive what you believe you deserve." - Tim

"I'm going to take my repression, and make it my problem. And this is what determines my life." - Tim

The gap between what we know intellectually and what we believe emotionally is where most self-sabotage lives. We can see the destination on the map, but our nervous system won't let us walk there because it doesn't believe we belong.

Research on "cognitive dissonance" confirms that when beliefs and behaviours conflict, the brain experiences measurable distress -- and will unconsciously sabotage new behaviours to restore consistency with existing self-concept. - Leon Festinger, A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance (1957)

On the Two Faces

"Everyone has two faces: the one they show the world, and the one they only show themselves." - Tim

This tension between the public and private self is one of the core identity challenges. The work is narrowing the gap between the two -- not by performing better, but by being more honest.

Carl Rogers called this the gap between the "real self" and the "ideal self," arguing that psychological health depends on reducing the incongruence between the two through authentic self-expression. - Carl Rogers, On Becoming a Person (1961)

On Being a River

"Be a river, not a reservoir. The difference between spitting out other people's thoughts vs creating your own. Going somewhere. Not going stagnant." - Tim

Identity stagnates when we become reservoirs -- collecting ideas, storing beliefs, never moving. A river has direction. It carves its own path. That's what thinking for yourself looks like.

On Agency and Story

"Writing gives you agency over your own story." - Tim

"Tell me the story of your story." - Tim

You are the author of your narrative. Not what happened to you -- you can't control that. But the story you tell about what happened? That's yours. Journalling, reflection, this vault -- they're all acts of authorship. The alternative is letting someone else write your story for you.

Research on "narrative identity" demonstrates that the stories we construct about our lives directly shape our psychological well-being, with those who create coherent, redemptive narratives showing greater resilience and life satisfaction. - Dan McAdams, The Redemptive Self (2006)
