Anxiety and Waiting

"It's a power thief. Waiting." — Tim

The Nature of Anxiety

We anxious few — the few, because we don't talk about it, and always feel as if we're the only ones sorting this shit.

We all process things as either anger, sadness, or anxiety. Anxiety is an inefficient form of emotion — you need to keep shovelling; nothing is sorted on the spot.

Neuroscientist Joseph LeDoux's research showed that anxiety activates the amygdala's threat-detection system, which hijacks higher-order thinking. Unlike anger (which mobilises action) or sadness (which signals loss and invites support), anxiety keeps the nervous system in a sustained state of alert without resolution — "a fire alarm that won't shut off." — Joseph LeDoux, The Emotional Brain: The Mysterious Underpinnings of Emotional Life (1996)

Logically, I'm not going to die. But I feel in danger. I know I have this all in hand, that I am in control of what matters. But my heart runs and my breath feels strange. I know what the next steps are. I know I can take them. I know it will work out fine. But I hate waiting.

Where It Comes From

The insecurity isn't always clear. But the root is feeling powerless — not being able to affect an outcome. The experience with cancer scans crystallised it: tell me I'm fine, give me more chemo, cut me open again... but don't make me wait.

Research confirms this: a landmark study found that people experience more stress from uncertain waiting than from knowing a bad outcome is certain. Participants who knew they would receive an electric shock showed lower cortisol and anxiety than those who had a 50% chance. Uncertainty, not the threat itself, is the primary driver of anxiety. — de Berker et al., "Computations of Uncertainty Mediate Acute Stress Responses in Humans," Nature Communications (2016)

It goes deeper than that — being bullied with nothing you could do, making friends then moving town again, being told you were naughty and feeling nothing could make you good enough.

So you construct your own world. One that proves those cognitions wrong with action. If I do good things, I'm a good person, right...?

The Trap

When people challenge that constructed identity — especially many people at once — it hits deep. Logically you know it's noise. Emotionally, it hits the foundations.

It gets hard when the conscious/subconscious lines, and the logical/emotional lines, are blurred.

Anxiety's Purpose

Anxiety wants to hold us back. It keeps us the same at best. At worst, the worst version of ourselves.

It robs us of power in the present — energy directed to fighting a misguided inner conflict instead of being focused toward action. Even humble progress. A stumble. A crawl. But progress.

We need to settle what anxiety draws upon, for that's where it is fed. It has its claws in us, but it is we who need to let go.

Viktor Frankl observed this in the concentration camps: prisoners who maintained a sense of purpose — even a small, concrete task — survived at higher rates than those who surrendered to helplessness. "When we are no longer able to change a situation, we are challenged to change ourselves." Action, however small, is the antidote to anxiety's paralysis. — Viktor Frankl, Man's Search for Meaning (1946)

The Opportunity

It's an opportunity to get better at being at peace with the process, and to trust that time is an ally. That you have played a good enough mid-game to produce a comprehensive checkmate.

I want to fight. I'm a good fighter. I'm not a good waiter. So this is where the growth is.

Tim's 2023 Crisis

From the journal entry of 7 August 2023 — a week that nearly broke me.

"I almost broke last week... I spoke to Col, and handed my claim management to him. That felt better."

"Anxiety levels are down to 7/10 — 9/10, down from 15/10 last Thursday. But still enough that I feel it spilling over into the anger bucket, and a bit into sadness."

"My normal coping mechanisms haven't been working. But I am just doing what I need to do. Step by step, day by day."

"I picked up the phone and spoke to a client today — first time in forever. And I didn't die."

On channelling the anger:

"If I can bottle the pissed off-ness and use it when needed, with love, alignment, and in the interest of the greater good, I could be on to something."

On the deeper fear:

"I think I'm too flexible on my standards, afraid to speak up. Afraid I'll lose my friends."

What this moment taught: The coping mechanisms don't always work. The constructed identity gets tested. But putting one foot in front of the other — step by step, day by day — that still works. And asking for help isn't weakness; handing the claim to Col was strength.

Research on social support consistently shows that the willingness to delegate and seek help during crisis is a hallmark of resilience, not weakness. Psychologist George Bonanno found that resilient individuals are characterised not by self-sufficiency but by flexibility — including knowing when to lean on others. — George Bonanno, The End of Trauma: How the New Science of Resilience Is Changing How We Think About PTSD (2021)

On Insomnia and the Noise

"There's that much noise in my head that nothing else can be heard." — Tim

"I woke up extra early, mind running." — Tim

When anxiety takes hold, it doesn't just steal the day -- it steals the night. The mind runs because it's trying to solve what can't be solved right now. The antidote isn't silence but managed redirection: meditation, walks, audiobooks -- anything that shifts the channel without suppressing the signal.

On Coaching Yourself Through It

"I need to up the meditation. Go for more walks, listen to more audiobooks. And actually force myself through a bit of my to-do list, instead of moping about playing poor me." — Tim

"When I get the opportunity to whine to someone, I probably need to keep it more feeling and less facts." — Tim

"It's a chance to be a better us." — Tim

The path through anxiety is not around it. It's through action (even small), connection (even vulnerable), and honest self-talk (even uncomfortable). When your normal coping mechanisms stop working, build new ones.

On Hope as a Choice

"Sometimes hope is a choice." — Tim

On Building From Hardening

"I don't think it was emotionally constructive to become so hardened to it all through my time selling cars. But it was professionally effective." — Tim

There's a distinction between resilience and numbness. The car sales years built armour, but armour has a cost. The question is whether you can be tough without being hard -- strong without being brittle. Anxiety exposes the difference.

Susan David's research on "emotional agility" draws exactly this distinction: emotional rigidity (suppressing or armouring against feelings) looks like strength but predicts burnout and poor outcomes. True resilience is emotional flexibility — the ability to experience difficult feelings without being controlled by them. Armour protects you, but it also imprisons you. — Susan David, Emotional Agility: Get Unstuck, Embrace Change, and Thrive in Work and Life (2016)

On Strength as Proof

"I wouldn't have started this if I didn't think I was strong enough. I knew they would fight dirty. I'm in the eye of the storm, and it's where it's roughest." — Tim

"I have a strong sense of justice. But it's not fun being the someone." — Tim

"No one would describe me as weak. Like grandma. Her strength is in me too. Sometimes she would feel crap. She's gone, but her strength is in me." — Tim

When anxiety peaks, it helps to draw on lineage. The strength isn't manufactured -- it's inherited. The people who came before you survived things that would break most. That's in your blood. Use it.
