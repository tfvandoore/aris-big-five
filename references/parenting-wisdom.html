Parenting Wisdom

The Big Picture

"Kids are a life multiplier"

"Many things are complicated, but loving your children unconditionally is not one of them"

"Being a father didn't make me scared, it made me brave"

Dad Lessons

"A solid burp can save you an hour of screaming" "Getting someone else's poo on you isn't a death sentence" "You get a new sense of perspective" "Don't worry about doing everything right, just do your best" "Show up. That's most of it. Physically, emotionally, be where you should be."

Research on father involvement consistently shows that a father's physical and emotional presence is among the strongest predictors of positive child outcomes -- more than income, education level, or parenting technique. Children with engaged fathers show better emotional regulation, higher self-esteem, and stronger social skills. — Natasha Cabrera et al., "Fatherhood in the Twenty-First Century," Child Development (2000)

Setting Expectations

"Don't have adult expectations for kids. And don't have kid expectations for adults"

"A good dad doesn't want their kids to cry themselves to sleep. A great dad will let them when necessary."

"Enough money that they don't have to worry, but not enough that they don't have to work"

"If you don't discipline your child, the world will"

"I bet you love your kids, but you also got to love who they could be. Hard choices now, easy life later. It's kindness, but not just kindness in the moment"

Long-Term Thinking

"When parenting, what you're doing and my response is not about today, it's about 20 years from now"

"Children grow their human capital, but often trade away their creative capital in the process"

Sir Ken Robinson's research on education and creativity supports this: "Every child is born an artist. The problem is how to remain an artist once we grow up" (paraphrasing Picasso). Robinson argued that the education system systematically suppresses creative thinking in favour of standardised academic achievement, trading creative capital for conventional human capital. — Sir Ken Robinson, The Element: How Finding Your Passion Changes Everything (2009); also his TED Talk, "Do Schools Kill Creativity?" (2006)

The Mirror and The Prism

"And then your kids start doing the very things you dislike about yourself. You get mad at them, unfairly. You are really getting mad at an extension of yourself."

"They were a mirror I never knew I needed"

Philippa Perry writes extensively about this phenomenon: "When your child is doing something that you find difficult, ask yourself: Is this actually about them, or is it about me?" Children unconsciously mirror their parents' unresolved issues, which is why parenting often becomes a path to self-awareness. — Philippa Perry, The Book You Wish Your Parents Had Read (2019)

"Kids aren't a mirror, they are a prism." -- Tim

They don't just reflect you back -- they refract you. They take what you give them and split it into something you never expected. The light that comes out is different from what went in.

On Providing

"You want to provide, you are a good provider. But there's lots of ways to provide."

"How much did the house cost? Two children that won't be born"

"Taking care of someone is great. But taking care of someone who needs to be taken care of isn't that great."

On Growing Up

"This will help you when you are an adult. The problem was, I was a kid."

"Am I a man now? A man is just a boy who is old enough to ask that question"

"Why am I holding myself back? Have I convinced myself that this is as good as it gets? What kind of example does that set for my kids?"

The Three E's of Parenting

  1. Empathy - Try to remember what it was like to be their age. What have they been afraid of, what are they worried about for the upcoming year. Don't write it off as "it doesn't matter". Listen, don't jump to solutions.
  2. Engage - Share some stories to show you really understand how they feel. Help them see you as a dad who remembers what it was like. Play with them at their level. Try to learn more about what interests them.
  3. Encouragement - Encourage whatever their interest is. What do they love about it, what would they like to do more of, learn more about?

This will help them feel safe, and will foster their ability to try new things.

Dan Siegel and Tina Payne Bryson's research on "whole-brain" parenting aligns closely with this framework. They emphasise the importance of "connect and redirect" -- first empathising with the child's emotional experience (right brain), then engaging their logical understanding (left brain). When children feel emotionally understood, their capacity for learning, exploration, and resilience expands dramatically. — Daniel J. Siegel & Tina Payne Bryson, The Whole-Brain Child (2011)

Raising Adults, Not Just Kids

"Don't raise good kids, raise good adults." — Morgan Housel

The goal isn't a well-behaved child. The goal is a capable, resilient, independent adult. Every parenting decision should be measured against the long-term outcome, not today's convenience.

On Staying Relevant to Your Kids

"As parents, we need to keep growing and push ourselves. We want to raise kids we'd be proud to call our friends. But if they do everything we ask and make us proud, but we don't, we won't deserve to have them as friends. We'll stay parents." — Tim

"These little people who you raised, sacrificed, prioritised for, are now adults with whom you'd like to keep relevance with. You want them to know that you are someone they can trust, who does what they say they'd do, and remains a model for their future selves." — Tim

"If we read instead of scroll, exercise and keep healthy when they're young, take a deep breath and show compassion to complaints -- the reward of lifetime best mates is in your future." — Tim

"Don't show them that when you have young kids you give up, and then turn it back on as they are less needy. You'll just teach them to not prioritise their kids when they are young." — Tim

On Providing

"Fathers need to provide, protect, and promote the house." — Tim

On Objectivity

"I only ask people who don't have kids for parenting advice. Because they are the only ones who are objective about it." — Derek Sivers

Once you're in it, you lose perspective. The emotional investment clouds judgment. Sometimes the clearest view comes from outside the arena.
