Legacy and Wealth Transfer

The 100-Year Wealth Goal

"One of the key objectives set by her family office is to have the same amount of wealth per person in 100 years as they do now." — Legacy

This is the gold standard of intergenerational wealth planning: maintaining wealth per capita across generations, not just passing a lump sum.

The Williams Group's research on intergenerational wealth found that 70% of family wealth is lost by the second generation, and 90% by the third. The primary cause is not poor investment returns but a breakdown in communication, trust, and shared purpose among family members. Maintaining wealth per capita requires governance and values transfer, not just financial transfer. — Roy Williams & Vic Preisser, Preparing Heirs (2003)

What Wealth Is For

"Went round the table and asked everyone to say what their wealth was for. 'My mother said that wealth is a means to education. My father said wealth is a means to problem-solving. My sister said wealth is a means to philanthropy.'" — Legacy

Each family member defining the purpose of wealth creates alignment and shared values across generations.

The Danger of Wealth Without Purpose

"Avoid the fate of all those who become 'victims' of their wealth: those who don't work, who can't find a purpose, who 'lose their humanity'." — Legacy

"Some families try to ward off this scenario by requiring their children to reach a senior level in an unrelated company before they benefit or become involved in the family wealth. Even so it can be hard to stop wealth - or the promise of it - from enabling or hastening self-destruction." — Legacy

"If you do too much for your kids, you build your self esteem by stealing theirs." — Tim

Psychologist Madeline Levine's research on affluent families found that children who receive excessive material support without corresponding expectations develop higher rates of anxiety, depression, and substance abuse. The mechanism: when parents solve all problems, children never develop a sense of competence or agency. Wealth without struggle can become a developmental disability. — Madeline Levine, The Price of Privilege (2006)

On Legacy Beyond Money

"A legacy is what you get instead of getting paid." — Tim

David Brooks distinguished between "resume virtues" (career achievements, wealth, status) and "eulogy virtues" (kindness, courage, integrity). He argued that our culture relentlessly prioritises the former while the latter are what actually define a life. The gap between how we spend our time and what we value at the end is the central tragedy of modern ambition. — David Brooks, The Road to Character (2015)

Timing of Wealth Transfer

"Leave money to your kids when it's most impactful. Not live til 80, and they are 60 and their die is cast. Between the ages of 24 and 35 means they can buy a home, send their kids to better schools. Once they're 60 they don't need it."

The optimal window for wealth transfer is when it can change a trajectory, not confirm one. A home deposit at 28 beats an inheritance at 62.

Die With Zero author Bill Perkins argues that the utility of money declines with age -- a dollar given to your child at 30 has far more life-impact than the same dollar at 60. He advocates for strategic "time-decaying" transfers: giving money when recipients are young enough to leverage it into compounding returns across career, housing, and family formation. — Bill Perkins, Die With Zero (2020)

Perspective on Time

A useful reframe for clients who think they have "plenty of time." The future arrives faster than we think, and the past was longer ago than it feels.
