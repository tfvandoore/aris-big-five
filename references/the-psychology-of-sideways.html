The psychology of sideways

The Psychology of Sideways

"Sideways isn't failure. But it sure feels like it." - Tim

The Plateau Problem

Everyone talks about ups and downs. The highs of growth, the lows of setback. But nobody talks about sideways - that strange, unsettling place where nothing seems to be happening. You're not falling apart. You're not breaking through. You're just... there.

Sideways is the plateau. It's the treadmill where the belt keeps moving but the scenery doesn't change. You show up, you do the work, and the needle doesn't move. It's the most psychologically dangerous place because it masquerades as stagnation.

George Leonard documented this in his study of mastery: the plateau is where most people quit, yet it is where the deepest learning occurs. "To love the plateau is to love the eternal now, to enjoy the practice for its own sake." The masters are the ones who can tolerate the flat line. - George Leonard, Mastery: The Keys to Success and Long-Term Fulfillment (1991)

Why Sideways Feels Worse Than Down

When things go wrong, at least there's clarity. You know what broke. You know what needs fixing. There's drama in failure - it demands action, it creates urgency. You can rally against it.

But sideways? Sideways is ambiguous. There's no enemy to fight, no crisis to solve. Just a quiet, creeping doubt: Am I wasting my time? Is this all there is? Have I peaked?

Psychologists call this "ambiguity aversion" - humans find uncertain situations more distressing than clearly negative ones. Studies show people prefer a definite electric shock over a chance of one, and certain bad news over waiting. Sideways triggers this aversion because the outcome is undefined. - Ellsberg, "Risk, Ambiguity, and the Savage Axioms," Quarterly Journal of Economics (1961)

That doubt feeds straight into The Shame Gap. The gap between where you are and where you thought you'd be by now. And when you're moving sideways, the gap doesn't close. It just sits there, staring at you.

The Illusion of Stillness

Here's the thing sideways won't tell you: you're not actually still. Underneath the surface, things are happening. Roots are growing. Connections are forming. The work you're putting in is compounding in ways you can't see yet.

Think of it like investing. The early years of compounding look like nothing. The line on the graph is practically flat. But that flatness is the engine. It's the boring middle where the magic gets loaded.

Morgan Housel makes this point about Warren Buffett: over 96% of Buffett's wealth was accumulated after his 65th birthday. The decades before looked like "sideways" relative to what came later. Compounding's power is invisible for most of its duration. - Morgan Housel, The Psychology of Money (2020)

Level Up Mindset says we always need to level up. But levelling up isn't linear. There are long stretches where you grind for what feels like zero XP. That's sideways. And it's necessary.

The Quiet Space Before the Leap

Every significant leap forward in life is preceded by a period that felt like sideways. The Slight left turn is born in this space - the subtle, almost imperceptible shift in direction that only becomes visible in hindsight.

Sideways is where you recalibrate. Where your ambitions get tested not by adversity but by patience. Anyone can push through pain. Can you push through boredom? Can you keep going when there's no feedback loop telling you it's working?

What to Do When You're Sideways

  1. Recognise it for what it is. Not failure. Not stagnation. A loading screen.
  2. Trust the process. The inputs are still working even when the outputs haven't shown up yet.
  3. Don't panic-pivot. The temptation is to blow everything up and start over. Sometimes that's right. Most of the time it's impatience dressed up as strategy.
  1. Look for the slight left turn. What small adjustment can you make? Not a revolution - a refinement.

"Setbacks happen fast. Recovery happens slow."

Seth Godin calls this "the Dip" - the long stretch of difficulty between starting and mastery. The key insight: winners quit the right stuff at the right time, but most quitting happens in the Dip, which is exactly where persistence would have paid off. The Dip is a feature, not a bug - it weeds out the uncommitted. - Seth Godin, The Dip: A Little Book That Teaches You When to Quit (and When to Stick) (2007)

Sideways is recovery's cousin. It's slow. It's quiet. And it's doing more than you think.

The Six-Month Lag

"I feel I'm six months behind where I wanted to be, but I have the pieces in place." - Tim

This is sideways speaking. The pieces are in place. The results haven't caught up yet. The six-month lag between effort and outcome is where most people quit - not because they're failing, but because the scoreboard hasn't updated. The work is working. You just can't see it yet.

The Furniture Problem

"It's moving around furniture in the house, and claiming it's a better house." - Tim

Sometimes what feels like progress during sideways is just rearranging. Real progress in the sideways period is structural - it changes what the house can support, not where the couch sits. Know the difference.
