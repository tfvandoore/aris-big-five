People Change

The Four Ways People Change

  1. When they hurt enough they have to
  2. When they see enough they're inspired to
  3. When they learn enough they want to
  4. When they receive enough they are able to

On Willingness to Change

"You can't change someone, but I also realised that I need to take some responsibility for never asking them to change. Perhaps I was afraid that if I asked them, and they didn't want to do it, that would have been a true rejection."

On Self-Deception

"You must never ever fool yourself, and you're the easiest person to fool."

"We see the world around us, and make up a narrative around it. Not the other way around."

"People at the higher levels of self-delusion are happier, but are not those who go the farthest. Because they never are clear on where they really are."

On Learned Helplessness

"Beware of learned helplessness. You associate with your excuses and feel a problem is permanent. You stop trying. Feel they don't have a compelling future."

Martin Seligman's foundational research demonstrated that when organisms experience repeated uncontrollable negative events, they develop "learned helplessness" -- a state where they stop attempting to escape even when escape becomes possible. His later work showed this can be unlearned through deliberate optimistic reframing. — Martin Seligman, Learned Optimism (1990)

The Five Lies We Tell Ourselves

  1. I know what I'm doing
  2. I can make him/her change
  3. I'd do it if I had more time
  4. Nobody would understand
  5. I need this to be happy

On Adults and Changed Minds

"If someone is an adult, you can't really change their mind. They need to change their own mind. So how do we help?" — Tim

You can't force change on an adult. The doors to their mind open from the inside. Your job -- as a leader, partner, friend, coach -- is to create the conditions where they want to open it. Ask better questions. Model the change. But don't kick the door down.

Motivational interviewing research confirms that people are more likely to change when they articulate their own reasons for change rather than being told to change -- the practitioner's role is to evoke the client's own motivation. — William R. Miller & Stephen Rollnick, Motivational Interviewing (3rd ed., 2013)

On Beliefs We Inherit

"People don't want information, they want their views validated." — Morgan Housel

"When people want to believe something, they'll fall for anything." — Tim

"A belief about yourself doesn't need to be true to have utility. Think -- kids playing make believe." — Mark Manson

Some beliefs serve us without being true. The useful fiction -- "I'm in the last third of my life" (procrastination killer) -- can drive behaviour just as powerfully as a fact. The danger is when unuseful fictions go unexamined.

On the Discomfort of Staying the Same

"The discomfort of staying the same eventually is trumped by the discomfort of change." — Tim

People change when the pain of not changing exceeds the pain of changing. That tipping point is different for everyone, but it always arrives -- if you're honest enough to feel it.

The Transtheoretical Model of behaviour change identifies this tipping point as the transition from "contemplation" to "preparation" -- the moment when the perceived costs of the status quo finally outweigh the costs of change. — James Prochaska & Carlo DiClemente, Transtheoretical Therapy (1984)

On Healing Through Experience

"Maybe you don't need more time to heal. Maybe you need more or different or varied experiences to show your nervous system a different reality." — Tim

Time doesn't heal. Experience heals. Sitting in the same room for ten years won't rewire your nervous system. Getting out -- into new situations, new challenges, new discomfort -- gives your body evidence that the old threat has passed.

Neuroscience research on "memory reconsolidation" shows that old emotional memories can be updated and rewritten -- but only when they are actively retrieved in the presence of new, corrective experiences, not through the passive passage of time. — Bruce Ecker, Robin Ticic & Laurel Hulley, Unlocking the Emotional Brain (2012)
