Explaining something is like playing catch

"The thrower adapts to the skill of the catcher." - Tim

The Metaphor

When you play catch, it has a lot to do with how good the person throwing the ball is - not so much how good the catcher. The thrower should hit the spot.

The thrower adapts to the skill of the catcher. The thrower can teach, coach, and encourage the catcher. They can start to challenge the catcher.

But if the thrower starts too hard - it's not the fault of the catcher they miss it, drop it, or don't see it at all.

So it is with explaining something. We need to hit the spot, at the skill of the listener. We add scaffolding if needed. We don't go too fast. We build together over time.

Why This Matters

Most communication failures aren't about what was said. They're about how it was thrown. The expert who can't simplify doesn't understand the topic as well as they think. The leader who says "I already told them" but wasn't understood hasn't communicated - they've just talked.

"The curse of knowledge" - once you know something, you can no longer imagine not knowing it. Chip and Dan Heath showed that this is the single biggest barrier to effective communication: experts systematically overestimate what their audience understands because they cannot un-know what they know. - Chip Heath & Dan Heath, Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die (2007)

"Three dimensional answers to two dimensional questions." - Mark Manson

Sometimes the throw is too complex for the catch. A three-dimensional answer to a two-dimensional question isn't impressive. It's a dropped ball.

The Scaffolding Principle

Good throwers (communicators) do three things:

  1. Assess the catcher - what's their skill level? What do they already know? Where are the gaps?
  2. Match the throw - start at their level, not yours. Hit the spot they can reach.
  3. Progressively challenge - once they're catching consistently, throw a little harder. A little wider. Build the skill over time.

This maps directly to Learning Principles - connect the new to the known. If you throw something that connects to nothing, it lands on the ground.

Lev Vygotsky called this the "Zone of Proximal Development" - the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with help. Effective teaching (and throwing) happens in this zone: too easy and there's no growth, too hard and the ball drops. Scaffolding is the art of progressively raising the difficulty as competence grows. - Lev Vygotsky, Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes (1978)

In Client Conversations

"Fight the urge to teach and provide solutions. We need understanding and connection first." - Tim

"First meeting is just about making the clients feel understood." - Tim

The biggest mistake in advisory work is throwing solutions before the catcher is ready. You haven't assessed their grip. You don't know where they're standing. But you've already launched a fastball. And then you blame them for not catching it.

Carl Rogers' research on effective counselling found that empathic listening - truly understanding the client's frame of reference before offering any input - was the single strongest predictor of positive outcomes. People don't hear solutions until they feel heard. Connection before content. - Carl Rogers, On Becoming a Person (1961)

In Avoiding the "Buts"

"Instead of 'Yeah I get what you're saying, but here's something better' - don't say but. 'Yeah I get what you're saying. If we follow it through, here's something better.' Or 'Yeah I get what you're saying, and here's something better.'" - Tim

"Yes, and. Not yes, but. Build upon what they said, not negate it." - Tim

Saying "but" is snatching the ball back mid-throw. It tells the catcher their effort doesn't count. "And" keeps the game going. It builds on what's already in play.

The "Yes, and" principle originates from improvisational theatre, where it is the foundational rule: accept what your partner offers and build on it. Research on negotiations shows that "yes, and" framing leads to more creative solutions and higher satisfaction for both parties than adversarial "yes, but" framing. - Kelly Leonard & Tom Yorton, Yes, And: How Improvisation Reverses "No, But" Thinking and Improves Creativity and Collaboration (2015)

The More Words Problem

"The more words you use, the less you say." - Tim

The over-thrower. Launching ten balls at once and wondering why none get caught. Simplicity isn't dumbing down - it's distilling wisdom until only the essential throw remains.
