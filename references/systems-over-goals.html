Systems over Goals

The Problem with Goals

"Have you ever set a soft goal? The type you give up within days. You get to the goal date, and think about it for a second, before ignoring the guilt. What made it soft? It was soft when you set it."

"If you're always rounding the final turn, you're going in circles."

"Defining what it means to score a goal in soccer tells the player nothing about how to win the game"

Scott Adams makes the same distinction: "Goals are for losers. Systems are for winners." The point isn't that goals are bad, but that they only tell you what success looks like -- not how to achieve it. A system is the daily practice that makes the outcome inevitable. — Scott Adams, How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big (2013)

Goals as Direction

"Goals should just be directions"

"A goal is a compass. But that compass must only guide your systems"

"We all have goals, even if you're not articulating them. So look at the systems, or lack of, and that will tell the story"

Process Over Outcome

"Optimise for process, not for outcome"

"Winning or achieving the goal is icing, it isn't the cake"

"How you get there is the worthier part"

Progress as the Goal

"There is no happily ever after. The good is on the progress, the work"

"Joy and contentedness comes from a sense of progress towards a clear outcome that is meaningful for you"

Harvard professor Teresa Amabile's research on workplace motivation found that "of all the things that can boost emotions, motivation, and perceptions during a workday, the single most important is making progress in meaningful work." She calls this "the progress principle." — Teresa Amabile & Steven Kramer, The Progress Principle (2011)

"20 steps in one direction vs one step in 20 directions. One is progress, one is busy"

Keystone Thinking

"Keystone habits, keystone goals"

"Complexity kills motivation"

Charles Duhigg's research on habit formation identifies "keystone habits" as the small changes that cascade into larger transformations. Exercise, for example, is a keystone habit because it tends to trigger better eating, improved productivity, and reduced stress -- not because of willpower, but because of the identity shift it creates. — Charles Duhigg, The Power of Habit (2012)

Winners vs Losers Have the Same Goals

"Winners and losers both have goals. If you go to the Olympics, what's the goal? How many achieve it? Everyone in business has a similar goal. Yes, you need a direction, but the goal itself isn't the main driver of success. We need to look at the behaviours that create that goal and drive that progress." — Tim

The Mountain Metaphor

"To get to a better solution, we first need to climb back down our current mountain. It's like we examine the base of lots of hills, and only start climbing the right mountain." — Tim

This is the cost of change — you can't summit a new peak without first descending from where you are.

The 24-Hour Escape

Once per quarter: goal setting and reflection. How is the year going? What do I want to do less of, more of? Rate satisfaction with areas of life. Can I trace good or bad outcomes back to a person? If so, should I limit exposure, increase, or remove? Think about stop, start, keep.

Systems in Practice

"Systems in place to help you follow through, and show you care enough to help you change. When it's hard, we'll be here for you." — from Dave Ramsay notes

"Personal finance is 80% behaviour, 20% knowledge. The problem is the guy in the mirror. If I can get that guy to behave, I can be fit and wealthy." — Dave Ramsay

"Knowing how to do a budget is actually different from doing one. Knowing you should spend less than you earn is different to actually doing it." — Dave Ramsay
