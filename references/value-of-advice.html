Value of Advice

Beyond Products

"Just super and insurance is lazy, no thought or problem solving needed. Are we really changing their lives?"

"Not risk vs wealth. Coaching, educating clients to lead towards better decisions, better behaviour, better outcomes."

Russell Investments' "Value of an Adviser" study (Australian edition) estimated that a good financial adviser adds approximately 4.4% per annum in net value to clients -- with the largest component being behavioural coaching and discipline, not product selection or market timing. — Russell Investments, "Value of an Adviser" (2023)

"Change products vs change lives"

The Real Work

"Tax and stuff, that's good advice, but not great advice. If you want to give great advice, you need to have a vision of your clients that they don't yet have, that you can help them see."

Nick Murray built his entire advisory philosophy on this distinction: "Financial planning isn't about money, it's about life -- and the great financial planner is an expert not on markets or products, but on the client's own unrealised potential." The adviser's role is to hold the vision when the client cannot. — Nick Murray, The Game of Numbers (2012)

The Behaviour Equation

Behaviour = Motivation x Ability x Prompt

The levers for changing client behaviour:

  • Increase motivation -- give them a strong "why"
  • Increase ability -- training, education, simplification
  • Decrease needed motivation -- build it into routine
  • Decrease ability required -- break it into smaller steps, outsource it, get help

This applies to every aspect of financial advice: getting clients to save, to stop overspending, to follow through on the plan.

This is BJ Fogg's Behavior Model (B=MAP). Fogg's Stanford research showed that when motivation is low, you must make the behaviour tiny; when ability is high, even a small prompt can trigger action. For financial advice, the practical implication is: make the first step so simple it requires almost no motivation. — BJ Fogg, Tiny Habits: The Small Changes That Change Everything (2019)

The Solomon Paradox

"The Solomon paradox — you take advice far worse than you give it." — Tim

This is why advisers exist. The smartest person in the room still can't see their own blind spots. The value of advice isn't intelligence -- it's perspective.

"The problem with the world is that the intelligent people are full of doubts, while the stupid ones are full of confidence." This pattern -- where expertise increases self-doubt rather than reducing it -- is the flip side of the Dunning-Kruger effect. Grossmann's research confirmed that self-distancing (imagining the problem belongs to someone else) improves reasoning quality by 20-30%. An adviser provides that distance structurally. — Igor Grossmann & Ethan Kross, "Exploring Solomon's Paradox," Psychological Science (2014)

What Good Advice Requires

To give advice you need:

  1. Aligned incentives -- your interests and theirs must point the same direction
  2. Full context -- you can't prescribe without diagnosis
  3. Competence -- the skill to act on what you know

"If you give me a bunch of fake information, my advice is going to be fake for you." — Tim

The value isn't just the plan. It's the system that keeps someone on the plan when life gets hard. Accountability and care in equal measure.

"Success is binary, it just doesn't care. If you do the right things, you get a result." — Dave Ramsey

No shortcuts, no hacks. Do the right things consistently and results follow. Success doesn't negotiate.
