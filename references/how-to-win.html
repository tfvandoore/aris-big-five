How to Win

A collection of principles for growth, discipline, and winning at life. - Tim

On Growth

  • The only person you need to impress is future you
  • The thing with growth is the more you zoom in the more jagged it looks. And the points are painful
  • Growth is painful because you become aware of your deficiencies
  • You can't have both growth and comfort. One is the price of the other
  • The only time you don't have hardship is if you aren't growing
  • Choosing to grow equals growing pains
  • Take people and expose them to things they are afraid of or avoiding -- we don't know the limits. If for ten years you avoid things you knew you needed to do, what would life be like?
  • To get to a better solution, we first need to climb back down our current mountain. It's like we examine the base of lots of hills, and only start climbing the right mountain
  • Competition helps us all be the best version of ourselves. It's not I win you lose. Competition brings out the best in each of us

Adam Grant distinguishes "givers" from "takers" in competitive environments: the highest performers are not zero-sum competitors but those who compete to raise the standard while lifting others. Healthy competition creates positive-sum outcomes. - Adam Grant, Give and Take: Why Helping Others Drives Our Success (2013)

On Effort

  • "This is hard" - good. That means most people won't push through it, which means more for you. If it was easy, it wouldn't be a goal. Being hard is what gives it the value
  • Nothing says stagnation like a comfortable entrepreneur
  • The work works on you more than you work on it
  • Sometimes starting is winning (walk for five minutes). Sometimes finishing is winning (building a bridge). Be clear on which is which and don't mix the two
  • You're allowed to overthink, so long as you are taking action
  • If you can walk out and be proud of what you put in today, then all good, go home
  • Now I just want to achieve things because it's fun to achieve things. There's no need to prove things to people

Cal Newport calls this "the craftsman mindset" - focusing on what you can offer the world rather than what the world owes you. The paradox is that by focusing on the quality of the work itself, you develop the rare and valuable skills that generate the career capital for the life you want. - Cal Newport, So Good They Can't Ignore You (2012)

On Intelligence & Learning

  • Intelligence equals rate of learning
  • You can think you're smart but if you don't change, you aren't smart
  • If your plan requires luck, it's a bad plan

On Identity & Character

  • Confidence comes from the past, not the present
  • With a new identity comes new priorities
  • Don't judge yourself by how you feel. Judge yourself
  • Preserving ego today means you have less humility to burn tomorrow
  • It becomes a gift you share rather than a gift you seek
  • Commit courage capabilities confidence -- in that order. The project you're not ready for right now? Commit to it first
  • Give yourself identity labels to hold yourself to something
  • Fault and responsibility are two different things. Nobody is showing up to fix your life
  • Be who you want to be. But to what degree do I need to participate in your self-image?

Albert Bandura's research on "self-efficacy" confirms this: our belief in our ability to succeed is built primarily from "mastery experiences" - past successes that serve as evidence we can handle what's next. Confidence is not a feeling you summon; it is a record you build. - Albert Bandura, Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control (1997)

On Motivation

  • Motivation comes from deprivation, so long as you can perceive the gap. Think thirst, hunger. The thing with wealth is the gap is psychological
  • "I've got everything I need" vs "I need everything"
  • Behaviour = Motivation x Ability x Prompt. Levers: increase motivation (strong why), increase ability (training), decrease needed motivation (routine), decrease ability required (break it down, outsource, get help)
  • What you feed becomes stronger. What you starve becomes weaker
  • Confidence motivates

This is BJ Fogg's Behaviour Model (B=MAP): behaviour occurs when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge at the same moment. The most reliable lever is making the behaviour easier (increasing ability), not trying to sustain high motivation - because motivation fluctuates, but simplicity endures. - BJ Fogg, Tiny Habits: The Small Changes That Change Everything (2019)

On Gratitude & Entitlement

  • I deserve nothing and I'm grateful for everything
  • Entitlement is the opposite of gratitude
  • The only people who like excuses is the person giving them
  • We need to be reminded more than we need to be taught
  • There are no new fundamentals. All the wisdom has existed for ages. It's the messenger we listen to. When the student is ready, the teacher appears
  • The self-help industry is grandiose narcissists selling to vulnerable narcissists in a codependent relationship
  • Patience is about the behaviours we do, not how we feel about behaviours

On Showing Up

  • If you show up on time, speak honestly, and treat others fairly, we will get along just fine
  • Life is hard. Believing it should be easy makes it harder
  • Feeling lonely doesn't mean you're alone
  • Play the game. Play the game of being the best you can be. To get through a day while staying on task. To play you must be entitled to lose -- that's what makes it a game, that's what makes it fun
  • Set systems not goals
  • Winners and losers both have goals. The goal itself isn't the main driver of success. We need to look at the behaviours that create the goal
  • We win or we learn
  • You win as long as you never quit

"You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems." James Clear's central argument is that goals are useful for setting direction, but systems - the daily processes - are what determine progress. Every Olympic athlete had the goal of winning gold; the system is what separated the medalists. - James Clear, Atomic Habits (2018)

On No Risk No Fun

  • No risk no fun. If it was easy, everyone would do it
  • It's going to work because I'm going to make it work
  • Give people the chance to reject you
  • You never have to prove you're brave to someone else, you just need to prove it to yourself

On Soft Skills

  • Soft skills are just things that are hard to measure. There's 100 little things inside each soft skill
  • Perception vs perspective -- perception has no advantage, perspective is the superpower
  • Strategy is allocating limited resources against unlimited options
  • Work hard if you must, and work smart if you can
  • An hour before 9 is worth two after 5

On Core Commitments

  • What is this person's core commitments? To feel free? Wanted? In control? Liked?
  • Anger is an influx of motivation, not aggression
  • The feeling of guilt that you could have done better by someone is a positive thing
  • Vision for self must be greater than the fear
  • Everything you want is on the other side of a few hard conversations. And a lot of people will spend their whole lives without crossing over
  • Your success is determined by how willing you are to have awkward conversations
  • I like going from zero to one. But I'm not that drawn to building a sequel

On Coaching and Self-Awareness

  • Solomon paradox: you take advice far worse than you give it
  • Are people looking for permission? Looking for an excuse that's reasonable and protects their ego?
  • The coaching approach: "You are right. It's not your fault. You will never be successful. Now what? They usually get angry. Great, the fighter showed up"

"Future me: I am the asset you are building, and I am satisfied with the effort you put in."

Igor Grossmann's research formalised this: when reasoning about other people's problems, we demonstrate greater intellectual humility, perspective-taking, and willingness to seek compromise than when reasoning about our own. The fix is "self-distancing" - imagining you're advising a friend in your situation. - Grossmann & Kross, "Exploring Solomon's Paradox," Psychological Science (2014)

  • We do what has been rewarded in the past and we avoid what we've been punished for in the past

On Mastery and Repetition

"Dedication to do the repetitive tasks at something leads to mastery. Then external reinforcement takes over. Mastery makes things more fun."

"If I'm feeling uninspired, not motivated, in a bad mood, but do the same thing anyway - the outcome is the same. It's the doing."

On the Cost of Excellence

  • The effort to go from good to great is massive. Go from 7 to 8? Ten times more work. 8 to 9? Ten times harder than that
  • How did your attitude/focus/prioritisation/personal development help or hinder yourself, and how did it help or hinder others?

On Self-Knowledge

  • 24-hour escape once per quarter for goal setting and reflection: How is the year going? What do I want to do less of / more of? Rate satisfaction with areas of life. Can I trace good/bad outcomes back to a person? Stop/start/keep
  • Are you being the most powerful version of yourself? What would my most powerful self do today? How would they respond to this challenge?
  • What habits, what weaknesses are holding me back from being my best self?
  • Find your balance across the 4 career variables (money, mission, work-life balance, learning). Note mutual exclusion at the extremes
  • Most people are best off maxing out on one variable for their job

The Motivation Formula

Dream x Perceived Likelihood / (Effort + Sacrifice)

  • Effort: what you weren't doing, that you don't want to do, that you need to do
  • Sacrifice: what you are doing, that you want to keep doing, that you need to stop doing
