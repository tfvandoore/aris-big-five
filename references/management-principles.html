Management Principles

On Responsibility

"Their failure is on you. Their success is on them."

"When we make a mistake, we make it as a team."

"A hierarchy is about responsibility."

The 1-1000/hr Rule

Write down everything you do in a week on cards. Separate into:

  • $10/hr work
  • $100/hr work
  • $500/hr work
  • $1000/hr work

Then outsource, delegate, hire, train accordingly.

"Give everyone a single number to focus on."

The IKEA Effect

"You value things the more you put work into them."

This applies to management - involve people in creating solutions and they'll value them more.

Research by Norton, Mochon, and Ariely demonstrated that people place disproportionately high value on products they helped create, even when the end result is objectively inferior. They named this the "IKEA Effect." The implication for managers is clear: participation in building a solution increases ownership of it. — Michael I. Norton, Daniel Mochon & Dan Ariely, "The IKEA Effect: When Labor Leads to Love," Journal of Consumer Psychology (2012)

On Delegation

"If it takes just as long to brief someone else to do a task as it takes to do yourself, just do it. Flag as a training item if appropriate."

"You can delegate but you can't abdicate."

The Two-Minute Rule

"If something takes less than two minutes to do, do it now."

This is one of the core principles from David Allen's Getting Things Done methodology. The logic: if the action required to process something takes less time than filing it away and tracking it, just do it immediately. — David Allen, Getting Things Done (2001)

On Multitasking

"There's no such thing as multitasking."

"Our brains are built for planning OR executing. Not planning while executing."

Neuroscience confirms this. What we call "multitasking" is actually rapid task-switching, and each switch exacts a cognitive toll. Studies show that multitasking can reduce productivity by as much as 40% and lower IQ performance more than smoking marijuana. — David E. Meyer et al., "Executive Control of Cognitive Processes in Task Switching," Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance (2001); also explored in Daniel Goleman, Focus: The Hidden Driver of Excellence (2013)

On Feedback

"If feedback offends you, that's your fault. Not mine."

Addressing Performance Issues

Can't just admit there's an issue. Need to determine:

  1. What are you going to do differently?
  2. What are the measures? How are they measured?
  3. How do we know we've bridged the gap?
  4. How can I help?

On Scaling

"Scaling without improving service quality will increase churn."

"When you're growing a business you put on muscle or put on fat."

Client Boundaries

"If a client treats you poorly, tell me. We can fire them as clients, you don't deserve that and we don't need them."

"It's really hard to know boundaries that have never been set."

On Venting & Mental Habits

"Venting is step one. It's like farting in a room, and everyone else feels OK to fart too. They all feel relieved and accepted, but the place smells like shit." — Tim

Venting can make a mental habit. Smaller and smaller things trigger the same routine. A 5-second trigger can cost 30 minutes. It trains our minds to consistently think negative thoughts. But only if we don't go past step one. Step two is fix something, take action, get perspective.

Research on "co-rumination" shows that repeatedly discussing problems without moving toward solutions actually intensifies negative emotions rather than relieving them. Venting feels cathartic but can reinforce the neural pathways of negativity. — Brad Bushman, "Does Venting Anger Feed or Extinguish the Flame?", Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin (2002); also Amanda Rose, "Co-Rumination in the Friendships of Girls and Boys," Child Development (2002)

Find a trigger, and observe what happens. Observe the automaticity. You'll find it's like an unskippable ad. Mental routines are like what hand you write with — you can change it, but it's hard.

Hiring Philosophy

Integrated from Stream notes on HR philosophy and team culture.

The Three Non-Negotiables:

  1. Be smart enough
  2. Try hard
  3. Don't be a dickhead

If those three are a given, then it's a question of how, not who. If you're dopey, don't give a shit, or are an arsehole, then we have a problem. But if we're all good there, we can bridge the gap.

Respectful Directness

Be respectfully direct. People don't have the energy, time, or desire to try to read smoke signals to figure out what you want them to say or do. Successful people separate emotions and logic. Just tell them exactly what you mean as succinctly and kindly as possible.

"Speak the truth with love. Or: open, honest, direct. Being direct is doing people a favour."

Expanded HR Framework

Be smart enough branches into:

  • Straight intelligence / way of thinking
  • How that fits to requirements of the role
  • Qualifications
  • Current skills / experience
  • Trainable skills (heavy weight on the "try" part -- can we educate our way through this?)

Try hard branches into:

  • What are their motivating factors?
  • Is motivation intrinsic?
  • Discipline: hitting deadlines, not letting people down, showing up on time
  • Are efforts adequate for expected growth trajectories?

Don't be a dickhead branches into:

  • Respect for others
  • Following instructions
  • Positive attitude

On Qualification vs Motivation

We decide whether someone is qualified or a good fit. But only they can decide if they are motivated.

"As your boss, I need you to be up to the task and on point, both physically and mentally. As your friend, I just want to help you understand what's going on."

  • "No" is a full sentence.
  • Mistakes are OK. Dishonesty is not.

  • All feedback is good feedback.

"But I don't want that to come at personal expense of anyone on the team."

The BBQ Test

"If two of our staff were at a BBQ and were really getting into work talk, what would it sound like to a third person?" — Tim

This is your culture test. If the answer doesn't make you proud, there's work to do.

On Perception vs Perspective in Management

"Your perception doesn't add any value -- it's your interpretation of the world, unique only to you. Perspective is observing from outside yourself, putting yourself in the shoes of the person you're dealing with. When you are thinking like them, you have an informational advantage." — Tim

Mindset Elevation (Stimulus-Response Gap)

Integrated from Stream notes on staff development.

We have stimulus -- then reaction or response. The difference is our mindset.

  • If we elevate our mindset above the situation, the gap brings the other person up.
  • If we are weaker, and are "below" the situation, that gap brings the other person down.
  • Both gaps are self-reinforcing.

"Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our freedom and power to choose our response." This stimulus-response gap is central to both Viktor Frankl's logotherapy and Stephen Covey's model of proactive behaviour. The quality of that gap -- whether we react or respond -- determines whether we elevate or diminish those around us. — Viktor Frankl, Man's Search for Meaning (1946); Stephen Covey, The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People (1989)

Technology Systems Philosophy

From the Diverger talk: Be realistic as to how much benefit a new system will have and how long it will take to receive that benefit. Prioritise functionality over shininess. Things that just work are better than things that are promised to work better. Good tech won't fix bad processes. Make sure your workflow is well mapped out and documented.

The people factor in system changes:

  • Be wary of change resistance and change fatigue
  • Take your team on the journey — their engagement is make or break, and it's their lives you are trying to improve
  • Account for 'softer' benefits lost — people trained and comfortable with existing systems, difficulty hiring/training new staff
