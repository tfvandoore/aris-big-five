The Giant Is You

A book concept by Tim: "The Forgotten Art of Perspective"

The Core Metaphor

"We are all giants. To an insect we're massive. To an elephant we're not. But just because elephants exist doesn't mean we aren't giants."

When we compare ourselves against others, we have a neck problem -- we only look up. We don't see that we are giants to others. Others we can help up (serve more, grow more).

Psychologists call this "upward social comparison" — we habitually compare ourselves to those above us, which fuels motivation but erodes satisfaction. Research shows that people who also practise "downward comparison" (recognising their advantages relative to others) and "lateral comparison" (connecting with peers) report significantly higher well-being and gratitude. — Thomas Mussweiler, "Comparison Processes in Social Judgment," Psychological Review (2003)

The Types of Giants

We are giants (huge in comparison to others) in different ways:

  • Financial giants - What you've built may seem small to you, but transformative to someone starting out
  • Lifestyle giants - Your daily normal is someone else's aspiration
  • Family giants - The love and stability you provide is extraordinary to those who lack it
  • Health giants - Your discipline and fitness put you ahead of most

The Paradox of Growth

"Accept you are a giant, see yourself as one. And to be a bigger giant you need to be big enough to take responsibility and be small enough to take baby steps in the right direction."

Two qualities to grow:

  1. Big enough to take responsibility
  2. Small enough to take baby steps

This is the tension -- confidence without arrogance, humility without smallness.

Jim Collins' research on Level 5 Leadership found that the most effective leaders combine intense professional will with deep personal humility. They are simultaneously "big enough" to make bold decisions and "small enough" to credit others and acknowledge what they don't know. This paradox — fierce resolve paired with genuine modesty — defines the leaders who build enduring greatness. — Jim Collins, Good to Great (2001)

Why It Matters

The perspective game changes everything. When you stop looking up and start looking around, you realise:

  • You have more to offer than you think
  • Serving others is a function of seeing your own value clearly
  • Growth comes from acknowledging where you are, not just where you want to be

Martin Seligman's research on "signature strengths" found that people who identify and deploy their existing strengths — rather than fixating on weaknesses — experience greater engagement, meaning, and achievement. Recognising yourself as a giant is not vanity; it is the prerequisite for contribution. You cannot give what you don't know you have. — Martin Seligman, Authentic Happiness (2002)

The Shame Gap

The Shame Gap

"The bigger the gap, the more it is filled with shame." — Tim

Building a Mansion vs a Shack

Don't think you'll see the form of your mansion for a long time. We see less return on our investment for a long time. When others have built their shacks, you won't have a foundation.

That can shatter motivation and breed shame. The shame exists in the gap.

You can't achieve your potential as long as you live in the shame gap.

Brene Brown's research distinguishes shame from guilt: guilt says "I did something bad," shame says "I am bad." Shame is correlated with addiction, depression, and aggression, while guilt is correlated with empathy and corrective action. The shame gap is dangerous precisely because it attacks identity, not behaviour. — Brene Brown, Daring Greatly (2012)

The Dizziness of Freedom

"Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom."

The gap between where you are and where you want to be is both the source of drive and the breeding ground for doubt. The key is to recognise the shame for what it is — a symptom of ambition, not evidence of failure.

Kierkegaard called this "the dizziness of freedom" — anxiety arises not from having too few options, but from confronting the weight of possibility. The greater your potential, the more vertigo accompanies it. The shame gap is the modern expression of this existential tension. — Soren Kierkegaard, The Concept of Anxiety (1844)

Acceptance as Step One

  • What you do isn't what you are
  • The only thing you can't change is who you used to be. So acceptance is step one
  • "I'm not going to say no big deal, but so be it"

Missing Milestones

"Missing milestones can erode self-esteem. But consider how those milestones are defined. How -- and who -- sets them?" — Tim

The shame gap widens when we measure ourselves against milestones we didn't choose. Society's timeline -- married by 30, house by 35, "made it" by 40 -- creates a gap that has nothing to do with your actual progress. Before you feel shame about where you are, interrogate whether the benchmark was ever yours.

"Social clock theory" describes the culturally defined timetable for major life events. Research shows that feeling "off-time" relative to these norms — even arbitrary ones — generates significant psychological distress, independent of actual life satisfaction. — Bernice Neugarten, "Time, Age, and the Life Cycle," American Journal of Psychiatry (1979)

Expectation vs Experience

"Fulfilment is no gap between expectation and experience. Disappointment lives in the gap between the two." — Tim

The shame gap is really just the distance between what you expected and what you got. Shrink expectations or grow experience -- either way, you close the gap.

The Conceptualisation Trap

"You might be able to conceptualise what you want, but emotionally you feel you don't deserve it." — Tim

"Your unconscious will only allow you to receive what you believe you deserve." — Tim

This is the shame gap operating below the surface. You can see the destination intellectually but your emotional self won't let you walk there. The gap isn't between where you are and where you want to be -- it's between what you know and what you believe.

Psychologist Gay Hendricks calls this the "Upper Limit Problem" — an internal thermostat that sabotages success when it exceeds what you unconsciously believe you deserve. The conceptualisation trap is the Upper Limit in action: you can see the goal, but your deep programming won't let you reach it. — Gay Hendricks, The Big Leap (2009)

The Gaining of Maturity

The Gaining of Maturity

"I think a sign of maturity is that you become ashamed of views you once tightly held." — Tim

This happens as we evolve our perspective and think for ourselves. Often times, our beliefs are really not ours at all, but birthed by someone else. That's not to say any one of them is inherently incorrect, but they should be thoroughly examined, explored and weighed.

It can be hard to do so with true diligence. After all, we are talking to the foundations of who we are. But that is the point -- who we are shouldn't be based on the views of another. We are unique, but somehow thwart such majesty.

Developmental psychologist Robert Kegan describes this as the shift from a "socialised mind" (where our beliefs are authored by our environment) to a "self-authoring mind" (where we take ownership of our own meaning-making). Most adults never complete this transition. — Robert Kegan, The Evolving Self: Problem and Process in Human Development (1982)

On Catching Yourself

"You have to try to catch yourself. Hold beliefs at arm's length. And see there are different ones you can choose. It all starts by thinking you might be wrong, even if your instincts are telling you it's right. If I were wrong, how would that look? It's cognitive flexibility." — Mark Manson

"In what way is this activity or habit supporting my values?" — Tim

Maturity isn't just shedding old beliefs. It's building the habit of questioning current ones. The mature mind holds beliefs at arm's length -- close enough to use, far enough to inspect.

Adam Grant calls this "thinking like a scientist" — treating your own beliefs as hypotheses to be tested rather than truths to be defended. The people who update their beliefs most readily also tend to be the most successful forecasters and decision-makers. — Adam Grant, Think Again: The Power of Knowing What You Don't Know (2021)

On Feelings That Change Over Time

"Feelings once felt change over time." — Tim

What devastated you at 20 might seem quaint at 40. What motivated you at 30 might bore you at 50. Maturity is understanding that your emotional relationship to events is not fixed -- it evolves, and that evolution is itself the growing up.

Psychologist Dan Gilbert's research on "affective forecasting" shows we consistently overestimate how long emotional states will last. We have a "psychological immune system" that neutralises negative experiences faster than we predict. What feels permanent at 20 is already fading by 25. — Daniel Gilbert, Stumbling on Happiness (2006)

On Living With Contradictions

"Just because you don't know how you function doesn't mean you are broken." — Tim

"Explains a lot but doesn't change a thing." — Tim

Part of gaining maturity is accepting that understanding your patterns doesn't automatically fix them. Insight is step one. But it's not the destination. The mature person can hold the tension between knowing why they do something and still doing it -- and not beating themselves up while they work on changing it.

This is the "insight fallacy" — the assumption that understanding a problem is the same as solving it. Cognitive behavioural research shows that insight alone changes behaviour in fewer than 15% of cases; lasting change requires repeated practice of new behaviours, not just awareness of old ones. — James Prochaska et al., Changing for Good (1994)

The big mountain

The Big Mountain

"Make sure you know what achievement looks like. Otherwise it will just pass you by." -- Tim

The Story

This was something Nick mentioned in a workshop:

A young boy looks out the window and says, "One day, I'm going to be like my Dad, and climb to the top of that mountain." One day he does it -- at say 8 years old. He's very proud of himself. Wow, I did it.

If he stopped there, that's what achievement would look and feel like. Wow.

He grows up a bit and is a teenager. One day he realises it is just a hill. He thinks, "Ahhhh, that wasn't anything. It was just a hill."

Then upon reflection, we recognise that it was a mountain when I was 8. I'm proud of it. I should have been proud of it when I was 16 too. Now I'm an adult, I should still look back at that and remember it as a cool moment.

The Lesson

"Remember it as a big hill. The big pineapple was big and scary. Don't let learned cynicism dilute what you were justifiably proud of." -- Tim

Achievement is relative to where you were when you achieved it. The danger is that as you grow, your frame of reference shifts, and suddenly the things that once felt monumental feel trivial. But they weren't trivial at the time. That 8-year-old climbed a mountain. The fact that it looks smaller from up here doesn't change what it was.

This reflects what psychologists call "hedonic adaptation" -- our tendency to return to a baseline level of satisfaction regardless of achievements, which causes past accomplishments to feel smaller over time. The antidote is deliberate gratitude and recognition of past effort relative to past capacity. — Sonja Lyubomirsky, The How of Happiness (2007)

The Cynicism Trap

This is Level Up Mindset in reverse. We level up, and then we look back at our earlier levels with contempt. "That was nothing." But it wasn't nothing. It was everything -- at that level. And dismissing past achievements is a way of robbing yourself of the confidence they should have built.

"Confidence comes from the past, not the present."

If you rewrite your past achievements as insignificant, you delete the evidence your confidence is built on. Every big mountain you climbed at 8 is a proof point. Don't tear them down just because you can see further now.

Carol Dweck's research on mindset shows that people with a growth mindset celebrate the process and effort of past achievements, while those with a fixed mindset discount them -- and this dismissal actively undermines future confidence and willingness to take on new challenges. — Carol Dweck, Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (2006)

Applied to Business and Career

The first client meeting that went well. The first deal that closed. The first time you led a team meeting without your notes. These were mountains. The temptation, years later, is to dismiss them. But the person who honours their past mountains climbs future ones with more conviction.

"Better past, bigger future." -- Tim

Applied to Parenting

Kids need to be allowed to have their mountains. A child's first school presentation is their Everest. Don't shrink it for them. And don't shrink it for yourself later either. Their mountains are real, and so were yours.

The Braces Paradox

The Braces Paradox

A metaphor for self-defeating training. — Tim

You get braces to straighten teeth, for a nicer smile. But while you have them, you train yourself to hide your smile. When you get them off, the training remains. It's been self-defeating.

Yet, people pretty quickly learn to smile again. They have a feedback mechanism — the mirror.

The mirror can be the most helpful tool, to not just look at the outside, but the inside.

This is a pattern that repeats everywhere: the process meant to improve us can inadvertently teach us the opposite behaviour. Self-awareness is the mirror that corrects it.

Psychologists call this "compensatory behaviour" — coping strategies developed in response to a stressor that persist long after the stressor is gone. The behaviour was adaptive once; now it's just automatic. — Robert Kegan & Lisa Laskow Lahey, Immunity to Change (2009)

Where the Paradox Shows Up

  • Career growth: You work harder to prove yourself, but the overwork teaches you to associate your worth with output. When you finally "arrive," you can't stop grinding because the training remains.
  • Relationships: You learn to hide your emotions to protect yourself, but when you find a safe person, the hiding is automatic. The emotional braces are off, but you've forgotten how to smile.
  • Wealth building: You sacrifice everything to build wealth, and the sacrifice trains you to never enjoy spending. The teeth are straight, but you still cover your mouth.
  • Anxiety coping: You build coping mechanisms that get you through crisis, but they become your default mode even when there's no crisis. The braces are off, but you're still protecting.

Research on workaholism shows that the drive to overwork often originates as a coping mechanism for insecurity, then becomes a self-reinforcing identity — people lose the ability to derive self-worth from anything other than productivity. — Malissa Clark, Never Not Working: Why the Always-On Culture Is Bad for Business — and How to Fix It (2024)

The Mirror as the Fix

"Not a pretty view in that mirror, is it, mate? -- when someone asks you why you aren't doing the obvious." — Tim

The mirror isn't comfortable. But it's the feedback mechanism. The braces come off, and the mirror says: "Look -- your teeth are straight. You can smile now." Without the mirror -- without Self-awareness, honest feedback, or someone who loves you enough to point it out -- the training continues unchecked.

Tasha Eurich's research found that only 10-15% of people are truly self-aware, despite most believing they are. The gap between perceived and actual self-awareness is itself a version of the Braces Paradox — we think we've taken the braces off, but we haven't checked the mirror. — Tasha Eurich, Insight: The Surprising Truth About How Others See Us, How We See Ourselves, and Why the Answers Matter More Than We Think (2017)

Privilege

Privilege

"Privilege is invisible to those who have it, and no one respects things that are easy."

The Invisibility Problem

That's the trap. The things you were given -- health, opportunity, education, stable family, the country you were born in -- become so normal that you stop seeing them. They become the wallpaper. And when something becomes invisible, you stop being grateful for it. And when you stop being grateful, you start feeling entitled. And entitlement is the opposite of everything that makes a person grow.

"I deserve nothing and I'm grateful for everything."

That's the antidote. Not guilt. Not self-flagellation. Just awareness. The simple act of seeing what you have and acknowledging that others don't.

No One Respects What's Easy

This is the second half of the equation, and it's just as important. When something comes easily, we undervalue it. The kid who inherits wealth doesn't respect it the way the kid who built it does. The person who was always healthy doesn't cherish their body the way someone who recovered from illness does.

Easy breeds entitlement. Difficulty breeds respect. That's not a moral judgement -- it's human nature. And understanding it is the first step to overcoming it.

The "IKEA effect" research demonstrates that people value things significantly more when they have invested personal effort in creating them -- a finding that extends beyond furniture assembly to explain why inherited wealth, health, or opportunity is so often undervalued. — Michael Norton, Daniel Mochon & Dan Ariely, "The IKEA Effect," Journal of Consumer Psychology (2012)

The Awareness Shift

Recognising privilege isn't about shame. It's about perspective. It's the same shift that Identity and Standards demands: seeing yourself honestly. Not the curated version. Not the victim narrative. Not the self-made mythology. The honest version -- which includes the advantages you didn't earn.

When you see the advantages clearly, two things happen:

  1. Gratitude deepens. You stop taking the basics for granted. Health, safety, freedom, opportunity -- these aren't defaults. They're privileges.
  2. Empathy expands. You start understanding why someone else's path looks different from yours. Not because they're lazy or stupid, but because they started from a different place.

Robert Emmons' research on gratitude shows that people who practise regular gratitude exercises report 25% higher well-being, exercise more, and are more likely to help others -- suggesting that seeing privilege clearly doesn't diminish you but strengthens both you and those around you. — Robert Emmons, Thanks! How the New Science of Gratitude Can Make You Happier (2007)

Privilege and Leadership

This is where it gets practical. A leader who doesn't recognise their privilege is a dangerous leader. They assume everyone had the same starting line. They mistake their advantages for merit. They build systems that reward people who look like them and penalise people who don't.

Emotional Intelligence requires self-awareness. And self-awareness requires seeing the full picture -- including the parts you didn't earn. The best leaders are the ones who know they're giants (The Giant Is You) and also know that some of their height came from standing on a platform they didn't build.

"We are all giants. To an insect we're massive. To an elephant we're not. But just because elephants exist doesn't mean we aren't giants."

True. But part of being a responsible giant is knowing which parts of your height are muscle and which parts are the platform.

Privilege and Growth

Here's the uncomfortable truth: privilege can slow your growth. When things come easy, you don't develop the calluses. You don't build the Resilience that comes from being forced to fight for something. The silver spoon can become a crutch.

Nassim Taleb argues that removing all stressors from a system makes it fragile, not safe. Overprotection and excessive comfort prevent the development of antifragility -- the ability to grow stronger from challenges. This applies equally to individuals, organisations, and societies. — Nassim Nicholas Taleb, Antifragile (2012)

The answer isn't to reject privilege. That's performative and useless. The answer is to use it -- deliberately, with awareness, in service of something beyond yourself. To take the advantages you were given and turn them into opportunities for others.

"Pressure is your privilege."

If you're in a position to feel pressure -- to have something at stake, to be responsible for outcomes -- that itself is a privilege. Not everyone gets to play the game at that level.

On Pessimism as Indulgence

"Pessimism is an indulgence, orthodoxy is the enemy of invention, despair an insult to the imagination." — Wade Davis

"A pessimist is what an optimist calls a realist." — Tim

When you have privilege, pessimism is a luxury. You can afford to be gloomy because your baseline is comfortable. The person fighting to survive doesn't have the bandwidth for existential doubt. Recognising this is part of seeing privilege clearly.

The Bottom Line

See it. Name it. Be grateful for it. Don't apologise for it. Use it well. And never, ever assume that your starting line is the same as everyone else's.

The Phrase

This Latin motto has survived centuries because it captures something every ambitious person knows in their bones: the path to anything worth having runs through everything you'd rather avoid. There is no shortcut to the stars. The difficulty is the route.

It would be cool to come up with something personally meaningful -- a version of this that carries the same weight but speaks to a specific journey. But honestly, it's hard to improve on. "To the stars through difficulties" is about as clean as philosophy gets.

Through, Not Around

The word that matters most is "through." Not "around." Not "despite." Not "after." Through. The difficulties are not obstacles on the way to the destination. They are the way.

"The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way." Marcus Aurelius wrote this almost 2,000 years ago, and Ryan Holiday built an entire philosophy around it. "You learn best and grow the most when there's struggle."

"No challenge, no change."

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's research on "flow" showed that the deepest human satisfaction comes not from ease but from challenges that stretch our abilities to their limits. People report their happiest moments not during relaxation, but during voluntary effort toward a difficult goal. The aspera is literally where meaning lives. — Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience (1990)

The Real Opponent

Everyone thinks the fight is out there. Against the market, against competitors, against the people who doubt you. But that fight is just noise. The real fight -- the one that determines everything -- is the one between who you are right now and who you could become.

Character Comes from Imperfections

A spin on Kintsugi.

The Art of Golden Repair

Kintsugi is the Japanese art of repairing broken pottery with gold. When a bowl cracks, they don't throw it away. They don't try to hide the break with invisible glue. They fill the fracture with gold lacquer and put it on the shelf -- prouder, more valuable, more beautiful than it was before it broke.

The philosophy is simple: the break is part of the history. Not something to be ashamed of. Something to be highlighted.

Applied to Character

We spend so much energy hiding our cracks. Concealing failures, burying mistakes, performing a version of ourselves that's polished and unbroken. But that polished version is the cheap copy. The real thing -- the version with depth, with weight, with substance -- is the one that's been broken and rebuilt.

"A scar is also skin."

Your scars aren't evidence of weakness. They're proof you survived something. Every failure, every embarrassing chapter, every time you fell on your face -- that's gold in the cracks. That's what makes you interesting, trustworthy, real.

Brene Brown's research found that vulnerability — the willingness to show your cracks — is the birthplace of connection, creativity, and trust. Leaders who share their struggles are rated as more trustworthy and effective than those who present a polished image. Perfection creates distance; imperfection creates rapport. — Brene Brown, Daring Greatly (2012)

The Imperfection Advantage

Resilience isn't built in comfort. It's built in the breaking. And character -- real character, the kind people follow, the kind that holds up under pressure -- comes not from getting everything right, but from getting things spectacularly wrong and choosing to keep going.

Think about the people you admire most. Not the ones who had it easy. The ones who had it hard and came through the other side. Their imperfections aren't footnotes in their story. They are the story.

Nassim Taleb coined the term "antifragile" for systems that grow stronger from stress and disorder — the opposite of fragile. Bones strengthen under load. Immune systems improve through exposure. Character deepens through adversity. Some things don't just survive difficulty — they require it. — Nassim Nicholas Taleb, Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder (2012)

"Strong character comes from hard times."

The Perfection Trap

The alternative to Kintsugi is perfectionism -- the belief that you should be unbroken. That if people see the cracks, they'll see you as less. But perfectionism is a prison. It keeps you small, because the only way to remain unbroken is to never try anything that might break you.

Psychologist Thomas Curran's research shows that perfectionism has increased by 33% since the 1980s and is strongly correlated with depression, anxiety, and burnout. Anxiety and Waiting

Anxiety and Waiting

"It's a power thief. Waiting." — Tim

The Nature of Anxiety

We anxious few — the few, because we don't talk about it, and always feel as if we're the only ones sorting this shit.

We all process things as either anger, sadness, or anxiety. Anxiety is an inefficient form of emotion — you need to keep shovelling; nothing is sorted on the spot.

Neuroscientist Joseph LeDoux's research showed that anxiety activates the amygdala's threat-detection system, which hijacks higher-order thinking. Unlike anger (which mobilises action) or sadness (which signals loss and invites support), anxiety keeps the nervous system in a sustained state of alert without resolution — "a fire alarm that won't shut off." — Joseph LeDoux, The Emotional Brain: The Mysterious Underpinnings of Emotional Life (1996)

Logically, I'm not going to die. But I feel in danger. I know I have this all in hand, that I am in control of what matters. But my heart runs and my breath feels strange. I know what the next steps are. I know I can take them. I know it will work out fine. But I hate waiting.

Where It Comes From

The insecurity isn't always clear. But the root is feeling powerless — not being able to affect an outcome. The experience with cancer scans crystallised it: tell me I'm fine, give me more chemo, cut me open again... but don't make me wait.

Research confirms this: a landmark study found that people experience more stress from uncertain waiting than from knowing a bad outcome is certain. Participants who knew they would receive an electric shock showed lower cortisol and anxiety than those who had a 50% chance. Uncertainty, not the threat itself, is the primary driver of anxiety. — de Berker et al., "Computations of Uncertainty Mediate Acute Stress Responses in Humans," Nature Communications (2016)

It goes deeper than that — being bullied with nothing you could do, making friends then moving town again, being told you were naughty and feeling nothing could make you good enough.

So you construct your own world. One that proves those cognitions wrong with action. If I do good things, I'm a good person, right...?

The Trap

When people challenge that constructed identity — especially many people at once — it hits deep. Logically you know it's noise. Emotionally, it hits the foundations.

It gets hard when the conscious/subconscious lines, and the logical/emotional lines, are blurred.

Anxiety's Purpose

Anxiety wants to hold us back. It keeps us the same at best. At worst, the worst version of ourselves.

It robs us of power in the present — energy directed to fighting a misguided inner conflict instead of being focused toward action. Even humble progress. A stumble. A crawl. But progress.

We need to settle what anxiety draws upon, for that's where it is fed. It has its claws in us, but it is we who need to let go.

Viktor Frankl observed this in the concentration camps: prisoners who maintained a sense of purpose — even a small, concrete task — survived at higher rates than those who surrendered to helplessness. "When we are no longer able to change a situation, we are challenged to change ourselves." Action, however small, is the antidote to anxiety's paralysis. — Viktor Frankl, Man's Search for Meaning (1946)

The Opportunity

It's an opportunity to get better at being at peace with the process, and to trust that time is an ally. That you have played a good enough mid-game to produce a comprehensive checkmate.

I want to fight. I'm a good fighter. I'm not a good waiter. So this is where the growth is.

The psychology of sideways

The Psychology of Sideways

"Sideways isn't failure. But it sure feels like it." -- Tim

The Plateau Problem

Everyone talks about ups and downs. The highs of growth, the lows of setback. But nobody talks about sideways -- that strange, unsettling place where nothing seems to be happening. You're not falling apart. You're not breaking through. You're just... there.

Sideways is the plateau. It's the treadmill where the belt keeps moving but the scenery doesn't change. You show up, you do the work, and the needle doesn't move. It's the most psychologically dangerous place because it masquerades as stagnation.

George Leonard documented this in his study of mastery: the plateau is where most people quit, yet it is where the deepest learning occurs. "To love the plateau is to love the eternal now, to enjoy the practice for its own sake." The masters are the ones who can tolerate the flat line. — George Leonard, Mastery: The Keys to Success and Long-Term Fulfillment (1991)

Why Sideways Feels Worse Than Down

When things go wrong, at least there's clarity. You know what broke. You know what needs fixing. There's drama in failure -- it demands action, it creates urgency. You can rally against it.

But sideways? Sideways is ambiguous. There's no enemy to fight, no crisis to solve. Just a quiet, creeping doubt: Am I wasting my time? Is this all there is? Have I peaked?

Psychologists call this "ambiguity aversion" — humans find uncertain situations more distressing than clearly negative ones. Studies show people prefer a definite electric shock over a chance of one, and certain bad news over waiting. Sideways triggers this aversion because the outcome is undefined. — Ellsberg, "Risk, Ambiguity, and the Savage Axioms," Quarterly Journal of Economics (1961)

That doubt feeds straight into The Shame Gap. The gap between where you are and where you thought you'd be by now. And when you're moving sideways, the gap doesn't close. It just sits there, staring at you.

The Illusion of Stillness

Here's the thing sideways won't tell you: you're not actually still. Underneath the surface, things are happening. Roots are growing. Connections are forming. The work you're putting in is compounding in ways you can't see yet.

Think of it like investing. The early years of compounding look like nothing. The line on the graph is practically flat. But that flatness is the engine. It's the boring middle where the magic gets loaded.

Morgan Housel makes this point about Warren Buffett: over 96% of Buffett's wealth was accumulated after his 65th birthday. The decades before looked like "sideways" relative to what came later. Compounding's power is invisible for most of its duration. — Morgan Housel, The Psychology of Money (2020)

Level Up Mindset says we always need to level up. But levelling up isn't linear. There are long stretches where you grind for what feels like zero XP. That's sideways. And it's necessary.

The Quiet Space Before the Leap

Every significant leap forward in life is preceded by a period that felt like sideways. The Slight left turn is born in this space -- the subtle, almost imperceptible shift in direction that only becomes visible in hindsight.

Sideways is where you recalibrate. Where your ambitions get tested not by adversity but by patience. Anyone can push through pain. Can you push through boredom? Can you keep going when there's no feedback loop telling you it's working?

What to Do When You're Sideways

  1. Recognise it for what it is. Not failure. Not stagnation. A loading screen.
  2. Trust the process. The inputs are still working even when the outputs haven't shown up yet.
  3. Don't panic-pivot. The temptation is to blow everything up and start over. Sometimes that's right. Most of the time it's impatience dressed up as strategy.
  1. Look for the slight left turn. What small adjustment can you make? Not a revolution -- a refinement.

"Setbacks happen fast. Recovery happens slow."

Seth Godin calls this "the Dip" — the long stretch of difficulty between starting and mastery. The key insight: winners quit the right stuff at the right time, but most quitting happens in the Dip, which is exactly where persistence would have paid off. The Dip is a feature, not a bug — it weeds out the uncommitted. — Seth Godin, The Dip: A Little Book That Teaches You When to Quit (and When to Stick) (2007)

Sideways is recovery's cousin. It's slow. It's quiet. And it's doing more than you think.

The Six-Month Lag

"I feel I'm six months behind where I wanted to be, but I have the pieces in place." — Tim

This is sideways speaking. The pieces are in place. The results haven't caught up yet. The six-month lag between effort and outcome is where most people quit -- not because they're failing, but because the scoreboard hasn't updated. The work is working. You just can't see it yet.

The Furniture Problem

"It's moving around furniture in the house, and claiming it's a better house." — Tim

Sometimes what feels like progress during sideways is just rearranging. Real progress in the sideways period is structural -- it changes what the house can support, not where the couch sits. Know the difference.

Money Life as Sport

"You don't get to choose the hand. But you absolutely get to choose how you play it." -- Tim

The Game

Your financial life is a sport. Not a spectator sport -- a participatory one. It demands training, discipline, strategy, and above all, showing up when you don't feel like it. Most people treat money like a lottery ticket. The ones who win treat it like a season.

There's a scoreboard, sure. But the scoreboard is an outcome, not a strategy. The strategy is in the preparation, the reps, the game plan. Investment Philosophy isn't about picking winners. It's about building a system that works over decades. That's athletic thinking applied to wealth.

Play the Hand

"Hand" -- the hand you're dealt. Poker players understand this instinctively. You don't get to choose your cards. You get to choose your bet, your position, your read on the table. Wishing for a better hand is the most common and most useless thing people do with money.

Some people start with nothing. Some start with a trust fund. Neither determines the final score. What determines it is whether you play your hand with discipline or blow it on emotional bets.

Vanguard's Advisor's Alpha research estimates that behavioural coaching -- helping investors stay disciplined and avoid emotional decisions -- adds approximately 1.5% per year to client returns, making it the single most valuable service a financial adviser provides. The hand matters less than the player's composure. — Vanguard, "Putting a Value on Your Value: Quantifying Advisor's Alpha" (2019)

"Good investors can do OK with bad investments. Bad investors can't do anything with good investments."

That's the hand. The investor matters more than the investment. The player matters more than the cards.

Training

Athletes don't wing it. They train. They have programmes, coaches, recovery protocols. Your financial life deserves the same respect.

  • Reps: Consistent saving is your cardio. It's not exciting. It's non-negotiable.
  • Strength work: Building assets, not just income. Strength Goals for your balance sheet.
  • Game film: Review your spending, your returns, your behaviour. Not to judge -- to learn.
  • Recovery: Having cash reserves so you don't panic-sell in a downturn. Your return on reserves can be the highest in the portfolio.

The Coach

Every serious athlete has a coach. Not because they can't do it alone, but because the Solomon paradox is real -- you take advice far worse than you give it. A coach sees what you can't see. They hold you accountable when motivation fades.

That's the value of financial advice. Not stock picks. Not predictions. Accountability, perspective, and someone who stops you from making the emotional play when the rational play is right there.

The Solomon Paradox -- named after King Solomon, who dispensed legendary wisdom but made disastrous personal decisions -- was formally studied by psychologist Igor Grossmann. His research confirmed that people reason more wisely about others' problems than their own. A coach provides the psychological distance needed for sound judgment. — Igor Grossmann & Ethan Kross, "Exploring Solomon's Paradox," Psychological Science (2014)

The willingness to lose is what separates players from spectators. If the outcome is guaranteed, there's no game. The risk is the point. Embrace it.

Here's where most people get it wrong: they compete against others. Keeping up with the Joneses is financial suicide dressed up as ambition. The only competition that matters is you versus yesterday's you.

Robert Frank's research on "expenditure cascades" shows that when top earners increase their spending, it creates a waterfall effect where each income tier below feels pressure to keep up -- leading to higher debt, lower savings, and reduced wellbeing across the entire income spectrum. Money life is a long season. There are wins and losses in every quarter. Money and Wealth Philosophy reminds us that wealth loves time. You can't sprint a marathon. You can't day-trade your way to financial freedom. You need to survive the season -- the full, boring, unglamorous season -- and let compounding do its work.

"Money isn't made in the buy or the sell. It's made in the wait."

Play the hand. Train every day. Compete against yourself. Trust the season. That's how you win the sport.

Explaining something is like playing catch

Explaining Something Is Like Playing Catch

"The thrower adapts to the skill of the catcher." -- Tim

The Metaphor

When you play catch, it has a lot to do with how good the person throwing the ball is -- not so much how good the catcher. The thrower should hit the spot.

The thrower adapts to the skill of the catcher. The thrower can teach, coach, and encourage the catcher. They can start to challenge the catcher.

But if the thrower starts too hard -- it's not the fault of the catcher they miss it, drop it, or don't see it at all.

So it is with explaining something. We need to hit the spot, at the skill of the listener. We add scaffolding if needed. We don't go too fast. We build together over time.

Why This Matters

Most communication failures aren't about what was said. They're about how it was thrown. The expert who can't simplify doesn't understand the topic as well as they think. The leader who says "I already told them" but wasn't understood hasn't communicated -- they've just talked.

"The curse of knowledge" — once you know something, you can no longer imagine not knowing it. Chip and Dan Heath showed that this is the single biggest barrier to effective communication: experts systematically overestimate what their audience understands because they cannot un-know what they know. — Chip Heath & Dan Heath, Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die (2007)

"Three dimensional answers to two dimensional questions." -- Mark Manson

Sometimes the throw is too complex for the catch. A three-dimensional answer to a two-dimensional question isn't impressive. It's a dropped ball.

The Scaffolding Principle

Good throwers (communicators) do three things:

  1. Assess the catcher -- what's their skill level? What do they already know? Where are the gaps?
  2. Match the throw -- start at their level, not yours. Hit the spot they can reach.
  3. Progressively challenge -- once they're catching consistently, throw a little harder. A little wider. Build the skill over time.

This maps directly to Learning Principles -- connect the new to the known. If you throw something that connects to nothing, it lands on the ground.

Lev Vygotsky called this the "Zone of Proximal Development" — the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with help. Effective teaching (and throwing) happens in this zone: too easy and there's no growth, too hard and the ball drops. Scaffolding is the art of progressively raising the difficulty as competence grows. — Lev Vygotsky, Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes (1978)

In Client Conversations

"Fight the urge to teach and provide solutions. We need understanding and connection first." -- Tim

"First meeting is just about making the clients feel understood." -- Tim

The biggest mistake in advisory work is throwing solutions before the catcher is ready. You haven't assessed their grip. You don't know where they're standing. But you've already launched a fastball. And then you blame them for not catching it.

Carl Rogers' research on effective counselling found that empathic listening — truly understanding the client's frame of reference before offering any input — was the single strongest predictor of positive outcomes. People don't hear solutions until they feel heard. Connection before content. — Carl Rogers, On Becoming a Person (1961)

In Avoiding the "Buts"

"Instead of 'Yeah I get what you're saying, but here's something better' -- don't say but. 'Yeah I get what you're saying. If we follow it through, here's something better.' Or 'Yeah I get what you're saying, and here's something better.'" -- Tim

"Yes, and. Not yes, but. Build upon what they said, not negate it." -- Tim

Saying "but" is snatching the ball back mid-throw. It tells the catcher their effort doesn't count. "And" keeps the game going. It builds on what's already in play.

The "Yes, and" principle originates from improvisational theatre, where it is the foundational rule: accept what your partner offers and build on it. Research on negotiations shows that "yes, and" framing leads to more creative solutions and higher satisfaction for both parties than adversarial "yes, but" framing. — Kelly Leonard & Tom Yorton, Yes, And: How Improvisation Reverses "No, But" Thinking and Improves Creativity and Collaboration (2015)

The More Words Problem

"The more words you use, the less you say." -- Tim

The over-thrower. Launching ten balls at once and wondering why none get caught. Simplicity isn't dumbing down -- it's distilling wisdom until only the essential throw remains.

Hold the Knife by the Handle

"Everything in life has a blade and a handle. Choose which end you grab." -- Tim

The Metaphor

A knife is useful or dangerous depending entirely on how you hold it. The blade cuts. The handle gives control. Same object, two completely different experiences -- and you get to choose.

This applies to almost everything worth doing. Every difficult conversation, every hard decision, every relationship conflict, every professional challenge has a blade side and a handle side.

Grabbing the Blade

Grabbing the blade looks like:

  • Reacting emotionally before understanding the situation
  • Taking feedback as a personal attack rather than useful information
  • Seeing a setback as evidence that you're failing, not as data
  • Approaching a hard conversation ready to win instead of ready to understand
  • Using past pain as a weapon against yourself or others

The blade hurts. And the irony is that people who grab the blade usually don't realise they chose to. It feels like the knife attacked them. But they reached for it.

Daniel Kahneman calls this "System 1 thinking" — the fast, automatic, emotional response that precedes deliberation. Grabbing the blade is System 1 in action: the reaction happens before the conscious mind has time to choose the handle. Training yourself to pause is training System 2 to intervene. — Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011)

Grabbing the Handle

Grabbing the handle looks like:

  • Pausing before reacting -- even five seconds changes everything
  • Treating adversity as a training ground rather than a punishment
  • Seeing The Braces Paradox for what it is -- honest feedback, not an insult
  • Using emotion as information rather than as a decision-making engine
  • Choosing curiosity over defensiveness

The handle gives you power over the blade. You direct it. You control the cut. You decide what it shapes.

On Power and Control

"You relinquish control not because you are powerless, but because you are powerful." — Tim

Grabbing the handle sometimes means letting go entirely. Not every knife needs to be wielded. Sometimes the handle is the decision not to pick it up at all -- and that takes more strength than swinging.

The Stoic concept of the "dichotomy of control" teaches exactly this: focus your energy only on what is within your power, and release attachment to what is not. Epictetus argued that most suffering comes from trying to control things that were never ours to control. Sometimes the handle is simply letting go. — Epictetus, Discourses (c. 108 AD); Ryan Holiday, The Daily Stoic (2016)

The Choice Is Always There

"Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our freedom and our power to choose our response." -- Viktor Frankl, Man's Search for Meaning (1946)

That space is where you choose blade or handle. Emotional Intelligence is really just getting faster at finding that space. Stress Management is building tolerance so you don't grab reflexively.

The knife doesn't change. You do.

The Knife in Leadership

"The knife is necessary. How you hold it determines who gets cut." -- Tim

The knife is truth. It's criticism. It's a hard conversation. It's the thing that needs to be said but nobody wants to say. It's the performance review that could change someone's career. The honest feedback that could save a relationship. The boundary that protects your sanity.

You can't cut through problems with a pillow. You can't build trust without honesty. You can't lead without sometimes saying the thing that makes the room uncomfortable. But the knife has two ends.

Holding it wrong in leadership looks like:

  • Delivering truth with anger. The message might be right, but the delivery destroys it. People don't hear what you said. They hear how you said it. They remember the wound, not the wisdom.
  • Using honesty as a weapon. "I'm just being honest" is often code for "I want to hurt you and look noble doing it." That's not honesty. That's cruelty with a permission slip.
  • Cutting without care. Feedback without relationship is just attack. If they don't trust that you care about them, every piece of constructive criticism lands as a threat.

Kim Scott calls this "Obnoxious Aggression" — challenging directly without caring personally. The effective alternative is "Radical Candour": caring personally AND challenging directly. Without the care, truth becomes a weapon. Without the challenge, care becomes empty flattery. — Kim Scott, Radical Candor: Be a Kick-Ass Boss Without Losing Your Humanity (2017)

  • Avoiding the knife entirely. This is the other way to get cut — by not using it at all. Problems fester. Resentment builds. The thing you wouldn't say becomes the thing that destroys the relationship anyway.

Holding it right in leadership means:

  • Say what you mean, not what you feel. The feeling is the blade. The meaning is the handle.
  • Earn the right first. You earn the right to give hard feedback by consistently showing up, by caring visibly, by investing in the relationship before you make a withdrawal.
  • Choose the moment. The right message at the wrong time is the wrong message.
  • Be direct, not brutal. Directness respects people's intelligence. Brutality disrespects their dignity. There's a line. Walk it carefully.

"Thousands of reasons to carry a sword, but only a few justify using it."

Communication Tips teaches that it's not the art of talking, but having others listen. And people listen when they feel safe — when they trust the person holding the knife is a surgeon, not a street fighter.

In Relationships

This applies at home just as much as at work. The conversations you avoid with your partner, your kids, your parents — those are knives you need to pick up. But you pick them up by the handle. With love. With vulnerability. With the understanding that the goal isn't to win the argument but to strengthen the relationship.

"Your success is determined by how willing you are to have awkward conversations."

The awkward conversation is the knife. Willingness is the handle.

"My job is to make you comfortably uncomfortable." -- Tim

That's the handle. Comfortably uncomfortable. You feel the edge, but you also feel the care behind it.

Recognised Sources

"The obstacle is the way." -- Ryan Holiday, The Obstacle Is the Way (2014)

Holiday's core argument parallels this: the difficulty itself, approached correctly, becomes the tool. The obstacle (blade) becomes the advantage (handle) when you choose to engage with it rather than be cut by it.

"Pain is inevitable. Suffering is optional." -- Haruki Murakami, What I Talk About When I Talk About Running (2008)

Pain is the blade. The suffering comes from how you hold it.

The Behaviour Equation

Behaviour = Motivation x Ability x Prompt

The levers for changing client behaviour:

  • Increase motivation -- give them a strong "why"
  • Increase ability -- training, education, simplification
  • Decrease needed motivation -- build it into routine
  • Decrease ability required -- break it into smaller steps, outsource it, get help

This applies to every aspect of financial advice: getting clients to save, to stop overspending, to follow through on the plan.

This is BJ Fogg's Behavior Model (B=MAP). Fogg's Stanford research showed that when motivation is low, you must make the behaviour tiny; when ability is high, even a small prompt can trigger action. Kids aren't a barrier to our happiness. They are a doorway

Kids Aren't a Barrier to Our Happiness. They Are a Doorway.

"They don't limit your life. They redirect it toward what matters." -- Tim

The Myth of the Barrier

There's a cultural script that says kids are a sacrifice. You give up your freedom, your sleep, your money, your body, your social life. And sure, some of that is true on the surface. But framing it as a loss is fundamentally wrong.

A barrier blocks you from something you want. A doorway opens into something you didn't know existed.

Kids don't take your happiness away. They take your old definition of happiness and replace it with something far deeper than you had the capacity to imagine before they arrived.

Jennifer Senior explored this paradox extensively: parents consistently report lower moment-to-moment happiness than non-parents, but higher levels of meaning, purpose, and life satisfaction. The distinction between "hedonic happiness" (pleasure) and "eudaimonic happiness" (meaning) explains why parenthood feels both harder and deeper. — Jennifer Senior, All Joy and No Fun: The Paradox of Modern Parenthood (2014)

What the Doorway Opens To

Wonder. You forgot how to see the world with fresh eyes. They hand that back to you. A puddle becomes an ocean. A stick becomes a sword. The moon is a personal friend. You didn't lose wonder -- you just stopped looking. They force you to look again.

Presence. You can't be anywhere else when a two-year-old is asking you "why?" for the forty-seventh time. Kids don't care about your to-do list, your portfolio, or your five-year plan. They care about right now. And in dragging you into their right now, they teach you something every mindfulness book tries and fails to explain.

Jon Kabat-Zinn, who pioneered mindfulness-based stress reduction, wrote an entire book on parenting as a mindfulness practice: "Parenting is one of the most challenging, demanding, and stressful experiences you will have. It is also one of the most important opportunities for deep inner growth." Children are involuntary meditation teachers. — Jon Kabat-Zinn & Myla Kabat-Zinn, Everyday Blessings: The Inner Work of Mindful Parenting (1997)

Forced growth. Nothing holds up a mirror like a child. Every unresolved issue, every bad habit, every shortcut you take -- they see it. They mirror it back. They become it. So you either grow up or watch them inherit your worst traits. That's motivation no self-help book can match.

"Children have never been very good at listening to their elders, but they have never failed to imitate them." — James Baldwin. Psychologist Albert Bandura's social learning theory demonstrated that children learn primarily through observation and modelling rather than instruction. They absorb what you do, not what you say. — Albert Bandura, Social Learning Theory (1977)

Temporal Love. The kind of love that knows time is passing. That this phase, this stage, this tiny hand reaching for yours -- it's temporary. And that temporality is what makes it sacred. You learn to hold things loosely and love them fiercely at the same time.

The Redirect

Before kids, happiness often looks like: more freedom, more money, more experiences, more of me. After kids, happiness looks like: this moment, this person, this messy dinner table, this bedtime story I've read four hundred times.

It's not less. It's different. And for most of us, it's more.

Parenting Wisdom isn't about techniques and strategies. It's about recognising that these small humans are doing as much for you as you're doing for them. Maybe more.

The Hard Part Is the Point

Yes, it's exhausting. Yes, you'll question everything. Yes, there will be days when you lock yourself in the bathroom for ninety seconds of silence and call it self-care.

But the hard part is the doorway. The sleepless nights build resilience you didn't know you needed. The tantrums teach patience you couldn't have learned anywhere else. The constant demands strip away the nonessential and leave you with what actually matters.

This mirrors what psychologists call "post-traumatic growth" -- the phenomenon where significant challenge leads not just to recovery but to development beyond the previous baseline. Richard Tedeschi and Lawrence Calhoun's research found that people who endure struggle often report greater appreciation for life, stronger relationships, and a deeper sense of personal strength. Parenthood is the most common form of chosen adversity that triggers this growth. — Richard Tedeschi & Lawrence Calhoun, "Posttraumatic Growth: Conceptual Foundations and Empirical Evidence," Psychological Inquiry (2004)

"Not every day is a good day, but there will always be something good about every day."

Kids make sure of that. Even on the worst day, there's a small hand, a silly laugh, an unexpected "I love you" that rewires your entire nervous system.

The Legacy

Family Values aren't abstract principles. They're the things your kids absorb by watching you live. The way you treat their mother. The way you handle stress. The way you show up when you're tired. That's the doorway working both ways -- they walk through it into the world, carrying everything you showed them.

They aren't a barrier. They never were. They're the reason the door was there in the first place.
